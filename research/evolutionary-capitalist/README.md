## Overview

~74 videos, very small audience (single/double-digit views). Reads prepared essays aloud — this is a writing project presented as video. Anti-libertarian in orientation, but constructing his own framework rather than simply critiquing from the left.

### His Framework: "Evolutionary Capitalism"

From video titles and content, the core ideas appear to be:

- **High Bidder Property System** — property should be allocated to whoever can make the most productive use of it, determined by competitive bidding rather than Lockean homesteading
- **Evolutionary Game Theory as Foundation** — property norms emerge from evolutionary equilibria, not natural rights
- **Anti-Privacy** — transparency is foundational to market function; privacy enables bad actors
- **Crypto-Reputation System** — reputation-based enforcement replaces government and violence
- **"Right Makes Might"** — inverts the typical formulation; productive capacity determines legitimate ownership
- **Anti-Praxeology** — rejects Mises' action axiom and ordinal utility framework
- **Taxation as Legitimate Rent** — explicitly rejects "taxation is theft"

### Recurring Targets

- Murray Rothbard (self-ownership, homesteading, NAP)
- Hans-Hermann Hoppe (argumentation ethics)

### Vault Relevance

His critiques overlap significantly with existing vault analysis:
- The self-ownership derivation chain problem ([vault page](../philosophy/legal-theory/self-ownership-and-property-rights.md)) — he identifies the tension but doesn't know about the conflict avoidance alternative
- The ancap stability problem ([Godel Governance](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md), Option B) — his "all land is claimed" scenario is a specific case of the power asymmetry problem
- His "high bidder" system raises its own Godel problems: who measures productivity? who enforces reallocation? This is the central planning problem in market clothing

He's independently arriving at some of the same conclusions the vault reaches through different paths, which makes him useful as a cross-check. Where he diverges (especially on privacy, reputation systems, and utilitarian property allocation), the disagreements are substantive and worth engaging.
