# Civilizational Cycles
> Theories of why civilizations rise and fall, how they connect to economics, and what they reveal about the drift from freedom to coercion.

## Overview

Thirteen thinkers across three camps, all describing the same meta-pattern: **voluntary cooperation -> success -> institutionalization -> capture by incumbents -> coercion -> collapse -> rebuild.**

## I. Cyclical Historians

Those who see civilizations following predictable patterns. Full treatment: [Cyclical Historians](./cyclical-historians.md)

- **Ibn Khaldun** (1332-1406) — *asabiyyah* (group solidarity) rises under hardship, dissolves through luxury. ~120-year dynastic cycle. First supply-side economist. Best single match for the vault's framework.
- **Spengler** (1880-1936) — civilizations are organisms with fixed lifespans (~1000 yr). Culture phase (creative) gives way to Civilization phase (mechanical). Most deterministic — offers no escape.
- **Toynbee** (1889-1975) — creative minority inspires through challenge-and-response; decline begins when it becomes a dominant minority ruling by force, not inspiration.
- **Strauss & Howe** — ~80-100 year generational cycle (High -> Awakening -> Unraveling -> Crisis). Generational memory fades; those born into prosperity treat the foundation as given.
- **Glubb** (1897-1986) — ~250-year empire lifecycle across 11 empires. Six stages from Pioneers to Decadence. Empirical backbone — consistent timing across geography, religion, technology.

## II. Structural-Institutional Analysts

Those who identify specific decay mechanisms. Full treatment: [Structural-Institutional Analysts](./structural-institutional.md)

- **Turchin** (1957-) — quantitative "secular cycles" (~200-300 yr) driven by elite overproduction, wage compression, and state fiscal crisis. Most rigorous data-driven framework. CMLS finding: war drove cooperative institutions.
- **Tainter** (1949-) — societies solve problems by adding complexity until marginal returns go negative; collapse is rational simplification. "Fixing 1 problem while breaking 99." Coase connection at microeconomic scale.
- **Olson** (1932-1998) — stable democracies accumulate rent-seeking interest groups that strangle dynamism. Only disruption resets institutional sclerosis. The political economy of the voluntary-to-forced drift.
- **Dalio** (1949-) — ~250-year Big Cycle driven by debt accumulation. Reserve currency enables extraordinary borrowing until debt becomes unsustainable. Provides the financial plumbing for other theories. Stage 5 debt squeeze now.

## III. Economic Theorists

Those whose market/knowledge insights explain WHY the mechanisms operate. Full treatment: [Economic Theorists](./economic-theorists.md)

- **Hayek** (1899-1992) — the knowledge problem makes central coordination impossible; each intervention requires another, ratcheting toward serfdom. Spontaneous order > designed systems. Economic backbone of the vault.
- **Sowell** (1930-) — constrained vision (human nature is fixed, accept trade-offs) vs unconstrained vision (human nature is perfectible, ideal solutions exist). The unconstrained vision is the intellectual driver of the voluntary-to-forced drift.
- **Schumpeter** (1883-1950) — creative destruction is capitalism's engine, but capitalism produces intellectuals hostile to it who vote restrictions into place. Democratic self-destruction, not revolution.
- **Kondratiev** — ~40-60 year technology waves. Installation phase (speculative) -> deployment phase (broadly distributed). We may be in Wave 5->6 transition (IT->AI).

## Synthesis: Where They Agree

1. **Success contains the seeds of failure** — luxury dissolves solidarity, stability accumulates sclerosis, prosperity produces elite overproduction
2. **The shift from earned authority to imposed control** is the key inflection point
3. **Complexity has diminishing returns** — each additional layer costs more and delivers less
4. **External disruption is the primary reset** — stability is paradoxically dangerous
5. **Trust is the foundation; coercion destroys it** — markets, institutions, and social order all depend on voluntary cooperation

## Open Questions

1. **Is the cycle breakable?** Hayek says yes (constitutional constraints), Spengler says no. Historical record favors Spengler, but n is small.
2. **Does AI change the equation?** Does AI's information processing address Hayek's knowledge problem, or is the knowledge too tacit and contextual?
3. **Is Turchin's CMLS finding fatal to libertarian hope?** If cooperative institutions require external threat, does peace guarantee their decay?
4. **Where are we?** Every framework points to 2020s-2030s as fundamental restructuring. Convergence or confirmation bias?
5. **What would constrained-vision institutional design look like?** Can AI preserve spontaneous order rather than replace it?
