# Did Christian Societies Actually Outperform? — The Comparative-Outcomes Question
> The one un-defused claim left by the [Wilson force-doctrine cluster](../../debates/wilson-atheists-benefit-christian-society.md): "Christian ethics produces the best outcomes even for non-Christians — even when corrupted." This page does *not* answer it. It frames it so the eventual empirics are clean — naming the metric, the confounds, and the mechanisms that would have to be isolated before any "Christianity → flourishing" story is more than just-so history.

**Links:** [Do Atheists Benefit from a Christian Society?](../../debates/wilson-atheists-benefit-christian-society.md) (the debate this residue falls out of), [Force Doctrine — The Theory](../morality/force-doctrine.md), [Wilson on Triggernometry](../../debates/wilson-triggernometry-christian-nationalism.md), [religion as wisdom-compression](../../notes/words-of-wisdom.md)

## The claim, in its strongest and weakest forms

- **Weakest (reject):** "Christianity produced ~2 millennia of Euro-centric dominance." False as stated. For its first ~1,500 years Christendom was a *backwater* relative to the Islamic Golden Age (8th–13th c.) and Song China. The divergence is **recent** (post-~1500, accelerating post-1750). Christianity was *constant* across both the backwater millennium and the dominance centuries — so "Christianity as such" cannot explain a change it didn't vary with. **Name what varied.**
- **Strongest (test this):** a *specifiable, Western-Church-specific structural mechanism* changed something measurable. The leading candidate isn't Wilson's monogamy→fertility channel — it's **kin-network dissolution**.

## The best specifiable mechanism — and why it's narrower than "Christianity"

**Henrich / Schulz (the WEIRD thesis).** The Western Church's Marriage & Family Program — banning cousin marriage, polygamy, and adopted heirs — dissolved clan/kin-based institutions → individualism + impersonal prosociality → the substrate that impersonal markets and bureaucratic states require. Properties that make this the *strong* version:
- It is **Western-Church-specific** (Catholic→Protestant lineage, **not** Orthodox) — which predicts the variance the loose claim treats as noise: why Orthodox Russia/Greece underperformed Protestant NW Europe. Under "Christianity = variable" that gap is unexplained; under kin-dissolution it's signal.
- It is **falsifiable** (cousin-marriage rates, kin-intensity indices vs. later institutional/economic outcomes).
- It is **not the monogamy→reproduction mechanism** Wilson reaches for — so confirming it would vindicate his *intuition* (Christianity carried a real structural win) while refuting his *account* of which win.

Adjacent variables that must be partialled out before crediting Christianity: the **Hajnal line** marriage pattern (late, nuclear, NW-European), Becker–Woessmann's **literacy/human-capital** revision of Weber, Roman legal inheritance.

## Periodization — the split point *is* the causal hypothesis

You can't date the takeoff without thereby committing to a cause. The candidate split points bracket the whole argument:

- **Classical (pre-Christian).** Credits Greco-Roman rationalism and law → Christianity incidental. *Killed by the Byzantium control (below).*
- **Carolingian / HRE (~800–1100).** Credits the **slow institutional fuse**: the Western Church's Marriage & Family Program (cousin-marriage bans → kin-network dissolution), the Papal Revolution / canon law (Berman, *Law and Revolution* — church-vs-state jurisdictional competition → rule of law), and self-governing universities/corporations. Causes medieval, effects early-modern.
- **Enlightenment (~1685–1815).** Credits the **secularizing cash-out** — awkward for the Christian-rule thesis, because the material hockey-stick (Industrial Revolution, GDP/capita takeoff ~1800+) coincides with the *disestablishment* of confessional Christianity from public reason, not its zenith.

**Most likely a relay, not rivals:** the medieval Western Church built the *substrate* (impersonal trust beyond kin, autonomous corporations, legal rationalism); the Enlightenment/industrial era *cashed it out* once printing, fragmentation-competition, Atlantic trade, and coal arrived. "Christianity caused it" is strong as **necessary medieval substrate**, weak as **direct cause of the takeoff** — so it is *not* ~2,000 years; the causal window is roughly medieval-fuse → early-modern-ignition.

**The control case that isolates the variable — Byzantium.** Eastern Rome held *Christianity* and *classical inheritance* constant but ran the opposite configuration — unified/Caesaropapist (emperor over church), strong kin networks, no aggressive MFP, no church-state jurisdictional competition — and preserved the Greek corpus for a millennium **without** producing the divergence. That rules out *both* "classical heritage alone" and "Christianity-as-such," and points at the *Western Latin* Church's specifically **decentralizing** innovations. The through-line with the HRE-fragmentation point: every operative mechanism *disperses* power and builds trust *beyond kin* — i.e. the success traces to **Christianity-as-decentralized-substrate, not Christian sovereignty.** The history credits the decentralization a unified-confessional state would suppress. (This is the sharpest form of the Orthodox-vs-Western within-Christianity experiment.)

## The confound wall (what kills naïve versions)

1. **Endogeneity / geography.** Europe's rise co-varies with fragmentation→competition (Hume, Mokyr, Diamond), Atlantic trade & New World windfall (Pomeranz), coal endowments. Disentangling "Christianity" from "happened to be Europe" is the whole Great-Divergence literature.
2. **Reverse causation / third cause.** Success → retain religion, or both driven by kin-structure/geography.
3. **Within-Christianity variance is the experiment.** Orthodox vs. Catholic vs. Protestant outcomes differ sharply. If "Christian" were the operative term, that variance shouldn't exist — it does, so the real variable is narrower.
4. **Period sensitivity.** Measured in 1300, Islam and China lead. The claim is really "post-1500 NW Europe," not "Christianity across history."
5. **Metric dependence.** "Outperform" on *what*? Population, GDP/capita (not aggregate — see GDP-as-welfare), technological frontier, longevity, well-being. The answer flips by axis — which is exactly the demographic-vs-technological split below.

## The trichotomy the debate gestured at (and the modern problem)

A clean way to state the live pattern — and where it breaks:

| Configuration | Demographic | Techno-economic frontier |
|---|---|---|
| Christian-derived West (historical) | sustaining | leading |
| Muslim-majority states | expanding | usually lagging |
| Secular high-income | **failing** (sub-replacement) | leading |
| East Asia (Confucian/Buddhist/Shinto) | **failing** (Korea ~0.7, Japan ~1.2) | leading |

**The HRE irony:** the "even corrupted, still works" case (a weak, fragmented, dysfunctional Christian polity that still did well) plausibly worked *through* its fragmentation — competing jurisdictions, no single censor (the Mokyr/Hume innovation story). If so, the load-bearing factor was **decentralization**, which cuts *against* Wilson's unified-confessional-Leviathan prescription. The success case argues against the policy.

**The modern problem:** fertility is now sub-replacement across *every* high-income society regardless of creed — secular Europe, Catholic Italy/Spain, and tech-frontier East Asia alike. That says fertility decline is **downstream of development**, not of secularization specifically — which weakens the monogamy-specific channel and *strengthens* the [Triggernometry](../../debates/wilson-triggernometry-christian-nationalism.md) question: has wealth/insulation **decoupled modern societies from the selection pressure that built the norms**, Christian-derived ones included?

## Demand-side — the religion-shaped need (and its serious rival)

The mechanism above is *supply-side* (what the Western Church built). A separate **demand-side** mechanism would explain why secular states fail to *replace* it: religion also serves an existential function — meaning, mortality-management, belonging, a sacred ordering of time — that may be **ineliminable**. On this hypothesis secularization yields not *no* religion but a *substitute* (Jacobin Cult of Reason; Soviet/NK personality cults + revolutionary eschatology; softer modern forms — nationalism, political tribalism-as-religion, wellness/astrology, fandom). The record favors "the hole gets filled." Pedigree: Durkheim (sacred/profane as functional universals), Becker / Terror Management Theory (mortality salience → worldview defense), Geertz (religion makes suffering *bearable*). Distinct from [wisdom-compression](../../notes/words-of-wisdom.md): that layer is religion-as-compressed-*norms* (what to do); this is religion-as-*existential-technology* (how to bear finitude).

**The rival — existential security (Norris & Inglehart) — and why it's a *mislabeled* mechanism, not a defeater.** N-I: religiosity is *demand-elastic*, declining where welfare/safety reduce the need for religion's comfort (so secure Europe is the secular anomaly). The correlation is real; the *interpretation* fails on a conflation. N-I measure **material/survival security** and infer a drop in demand for **ontological security** (the "why am I here / what happens when I die" need) — but those are different needs, and satisfying the first *unmasks* rather than sates the second. What secularizes under affluence is religion-*as-survival-insurance* (the foxhole/safety-net function); religion-*as-meaning* is left intact to re-route. The vault's defeater is **praxeology**: action springs from ineradicable *felt uneasiness*, and the want-hierarchy is **unbounded** — satisfying lower wants *reveals* higher ones (Maslow's transcendence sits *above* security). N-I model need as a *depletable stock* security drains; it's a *renewable hierarchy* security promotes you *up*. Relaxing the material constraint doesn't slacken the system — it **re-binds the constraint on the meaning axis** (binding-constraint-determines-depth: the constraint moves, it doesn't slacken). Empirical wedge: **satisfaction ≠ meaning** — Oishi & Diener find *meaning in life* is **higher** in poorer/more-religious nations (religiosity-mediated) even where life-*satisfaction* runs the other way. Security buys satisfaction, not meaning. Signature of the unmet need at the *top* of the gradient: affluent restlessness/debauchery/addiction — *intensity substituted for transcendence* — the same fingerprint as sub-replacement fertility at scale, money mis-applied to a need money can't answer.

**Testable bridge to the demographics:** if substitutes *under-deliver* on the function, the failure should be measurable — sub-replacement fertility, deaths of despair, collapsing social trust, the "meaning crisis." Sharp version: people without a transcendent *why* may simply not reproduce, making fertility collapse the demand-side's *fingerprint*, not just a development side-effect. The "successful secular" Nordics then aren't a survived meaning-vacuum but **post-Christian residue + social-democratic civil religion** (borrowed capital — the [Triggernometry](../../debates/wilson-triggernometry-christian-nationalism.md) "is the selection still operating?" question). Note the policy upshot still cuts the same way: *disestablishment* (decline to crown a state church) is not *militant laïcité* (suppress religion); the failed cases are the latter, and "legislating the function out" is the bad-structure move — but so is mandating one carrier by force.

**Falsifiability guard:** keep an *independent* definition of religion (or pin the functional bundle), or "anything that fills the function counts as religion" makes "religion is universal" true by definition and explanatorily empty. The claim worth testing is *comparative*: do ersatz religions underperform time-tested ones on the structural metrics?

**Corollary — "remove material wants → paradise" is the depletable-stock fallacy at civilizational scale.** The unbounded-need point refutes materialist utopianism for *rigorous* reasons: (a) **irreducible scarcity** — even in a land of Cockaigne, *time* and the singular life stay scarce, so choice and conflict survive material superabundance (Mises: abundance of stuff never abolishes the economizing of time); (b) **positional goods** — status/rank/mates are defined by *relative* position, zero-sum by construction, so growth can't satiate them, and visible-but-inaccessible abundance *manufactures* new relative-deprivation suffering a poorer reference-class never felt (Hirsch, Frank); (c) **hedonic reference-reset** — gains normalize against the contemporary benchmark, so a US poor person ~orders of magnitude richer than an average ancient Roman isn't ~orders happier (Easterlin's relative channel). **Scope guard** (so the point doesn't overshoot into the false/callous "the poor have it great"): at the curve's *bottom* — famine, child mortality, untreated disease — material provision is the highest-leverage lever and the egalitarian is right; the error is [scope confusion](../morality/scope-confusion.md), universalizing a true claim about the bottom into a false one about the whole curve. Returns *bend*; above the bend the binding constraints are positional + existential, which growth can't touch. **Danger:** a materialist utopianism is itself a substitute religion whose eschatology — the need being unbounded — never arrives, so it defaults to "saboteurs explain the gap" → escalating coercion (Voegelin's *immanentize the eschaton*; the 20th-c. body count). Removing the time-tested carrier installed a worse, brake-less one.

## The actual open question

Not "which faith wins" but: **is the both-at-once configuration (demographic sustainability × technological frontier) a stable attractor at all — or does development always erode the demographic engine, creed notwithstanding?** The debate's own data (Christian-descended West is *also* sub-replacement) points at "no stable attractor yet," which would reframe the Christian advantage as a *transitional historical phase*, not a durable equilibrium — consistent with [Force Doctrine](../morality/force-doctrine.md)'s "structure wins the war, but the current structures may all be decaying on the fertility axis."

## What would actually settle it (deferred — the empirics)

A real comparative study: kin-intensity / cousin-marriage indices, period-stratified outcome panels (don't pool pre- and post-1500), within-Christianity contrasts (Orthodox/Catholic/Protestant), development-controlled fertility regressions, and an explicit objective function (which metric, over what horizon). Candidate for a future [deep-research](../../../) run. Until then this stays an **open question**, not a vault conclusion.
