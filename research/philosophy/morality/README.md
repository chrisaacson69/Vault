# Morality — First Principles Exploration
> Building a naturalistic moral framework from bedrock concepts upward.

**Links:** [Philosophy](../README.md), [Economics — Value and Profit](../../economics/value-and-profit.md), [The Limits of Utopia](../../../notes/limits-of-utopia.md), [Scope Confusion](./scope-confusion.md)

## Starting Definitions

Three overlapping circles, scaling from individual to institutional:

- **Morality** — right and wrong at the level of the individual conscience.
- **Ethics** — group norms of right and wrong.
- **Law** — codified ethics with legal consequences.

These overlap, not nest. There are moral truths no group has recognized, group norms never codified, and laws with no moral basis.

## Prerequisites for Morality

Morality requires three conditions:

1. **Physical reality** — actions have consequences determined by causal structure.
2. **Consciousness** — outcomes are *experienced*, not just occurring.
3. **Agency (choice)** — the capacity to select between actions. Without choice, morality is meaningless.

**Implication:** This framework *depends* on free will being real. That dependency is accepted. "Ought implies can" (Kant).

## Core Arguments

### The Is-Ought Bridge
The is-ought gap dissolves through **performative grounding** — the questioner's own existence, consciousness, and agency are brute facts that presuppose valuation. Denying them requires using them. Full argument: [Is-Ought and Performative Grounding](./is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md).

### Action/Inaction Asymmetry
Action is singular and defined; inaction is the infinite complement. This grounds the negative/positive rights distinction and the coercion boundary. Full argument: [Action/Inaction Asymmetry](./action-inaction-asymmetry.md).

### Moral Content
Morality is *probabilistic heuristics*, discovered empirically across millennia. Key claims:
- Religious and cultural norms encode survival wisdom (cached consequentialism)
- Cross-cultural convergence is evidence for objectivity (independent discovery of similar principles)
- History is error-correcting (immoral strategies fail on civilizational timescales)
- Morality is probabilistic, not absolute (expected value over lifetimes, not certainty per choice)
- Empirics come first; given enough data, the logical structure reveals itself. Performative grounding is the formalization, not a separate path.

### Two Layers of Good and Bad

Action (praxeology) and morality overlap but aren't identical. There are two evaluation layers:

| Layer | "Good" means | "Bad" means | Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Praxeological** | Purpose fulfilled | Purpose not fulfilled | Individual — did the action achieve its end? |
| **Constitutive/Moral** | Action sustains conditions for agency | Action degrades conditions for agency | Structural — does it work for everyone? |

The praxeological layer is real and matters — putting on shoes successfully IS good in the means-ends sense. But it's not sufficient for morality. Successful theft is praxeologically good (the thief achieved their end) and constitutively bad (undermines the property structure enabling all agents to plan and trade).

Morality lives in the constitutive layer: not "did you achieve your purpose" but "does your purpose, when pursued, sustain or degrade the conditions under which agents can function?" This is why murder is wrong even when the murderer succeeds. The effect on the agency-enabling structure, not the success of the individual action, determines moral content.

Conflating the two layers — reducing morality to praxeological success — is how you get "the ends justify the means." The [scope confusion thesis](./scope-confusion.md) applies: praxeological and moral evaluations operate at different scopes.

**Open question:** If LLMs act in the praxeological sense ([LLMs as Praxeological Actors](../../economics/llm-praxeology.md)), do their actions touch the constitutive layer? An agent refusing to help with harm might. An agent managing inventory probably doesn't. The gradient matters. This question requires further thought.

See: [Constitutive/Elective Distinction](./constitutive-elective.md), [LLMs as Praxeological Actors](../../economics/llm-praxeology.md), [Relational Objectivity](../epistemology/relational-objectivity.md)

### The Scope Model
Moral principles are real but scope-dependent. The deeper the scope, the more stable the principle:

| Scope | Example | Stability |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| **Narrow** | "Don't be promiscuous" | Updates with technology |
| **Medium** | "Nurture your children" | Persists — human development unchanged |
| **Broad** | "Care for the vulnerable" | Very stable |
| **Cosmic** | No agents | No morality. Just physics. |

Change the creature, change the morality. This is naturalistic, not Platonic. For the scope-shifting problem, see [Scope Confusion](./scope-confusion.md).
