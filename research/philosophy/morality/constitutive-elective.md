# The Constitutive/Elective Distinction — Replacing Objective vs Subjective
> The objective/subjective debate is fought on a single axis that doesn't exist. There are two axes, not one. Morality is mind-dependent AND constrained — a combination the traditional vocabulary has no word for.

**Links:** [The Four Trunks](../the-four-trunks.md), [Morality](./README.md), [Relational Objectivity](../epistemology/relational-objectivity.md), [Stance-Independence Retreat](../epistemology/stance-independence-retreat.md), [Scope Confusion](./scope-confusion.md), [Is-Ought and Performative Grounding](./is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md), [Open Problems](./open-problems.md), [Noerr vs Terry X](../../debates/noerr-teryx-subjective-objective-morality.md), [Logic and Mathematics](../logic-and-math/README.md)

**Trunk:** This page states [Trunk 1 — Normative Ontology](../the-four-trunks.md#trunk-1--normative-ontology) of the vault's philosophy. The working name for the trunk's position is *constitutive realism*.

## The Problem

Every debate about moral objectivity runs into the same impasse:

- **"Objective"** is used to mean both *mind-independent* (ontological) and *not arbitrary* (epistemic)
- **"Subjective"** is used to mean both *mind-dependent* (ontological) and *arbitrary* (epistemic)

These are two separate questions collapsed into one word. The collapse creates a false dichotomy where morality must be either:
- Mind-independent (like the mass of the sun) — which is incoherent, because morality is about action, and action requires minds
- Arbitrary (like ice cream preference) — which is empirically false, because civilizations converge on core norms and those that violate them collapse

The debate is stuck because the vocabulary is broken, not because the question is unanswerable.

## The Two Axes

**Axis 1: Does it require minds?**
- Mind-independent — exists without any minds (mass, chemistry, height relations)
- Mind-dependent — requires minds to exist (morality, nutrition, danger, ice cream preference)

**Axis 2: Is it constitutive or elective?**
- Constitutive — structurally necessary; couldn't be otherwise given what the relata are; X can't exist or function without it
- Elective — optional; could be different without changing what the thing fundamentally is; varies by individual

| | **Constitutive** | **Elective** |
|---|---|---|
| **Mind-independent** | Mass, chemistry, gravitational relationships | (empty — if it's mind-independent and structural, it's just physics) |
| **Mind-dependent** | **Morality, nutrition, danger** | Ice cream preference, fashion, aesthetic taste |

The missing cell — mind-dependent but constitutive — is where morality lives. The traditional vocabulary has no word for it because "objective" was already taken by the top row and "subjective" was taken by the bottom-right.

## Why "Constitutive" and "Elective"

### Terms that don't work

- **"Grounded" vs "ungrounded"** — presupposes the conclusion. Calling morality "grounded" already asserts that it's constrained by reality. The opponent can say "I don't agree it's grounded — that's what we're debating."
- **"Convergent" vs "divergent"** — presupposes convergence. The theist can say "it converges because God made it so." The term smuggles in the mechanism.
- **"Objective" vs "subjective"** — the words we're trying to replace. They conflate the two axes.

### Why constitutive/elective works

**Constitutive** describes a structural relationship, not an outcome. Something is constitutive of X if X can't exist or function without it. This is a testable, debatable claim that doesn't pre-load the answer:

- "Is morality constitutive of agency?" — you can argue yes or no without the terminology biasing the answer
- A theist can say "yes, because God designed agents that way"
- A naturalist can say "yes, because the structure of agency generates it"
- A moral anti-realist can say "no, morality is elective — agents function fine without it"

The term is **neutral on the source** of the constitutive relationship. It names the structural claim without asserting the mechanism.

**Elective** immediately communicates: optional, a matter of choice, no structural necessity. "Is your preference for jazz constitutive of who you are, or elective?" Obviously elective — you'd still be you with different musical taste. "Is the harmfulness of betrayal constitutive of social agency, or elective?" That's the real debate.

## The Reframe

Instead of asking "Is morality objective or subjective?" — a question that can't be answered because the terms are broken — ask two separate questions:

### Question 1: Is morality mind-dependent?

**Yes.** This is not a concession. It's a structural fact:
- Can you steal without a mind? No.
- Can you murder without a mind? No.
- Can you betray without a mind? No.
- Can you perform any moral or immoral act without a mind? No.

Morality is about action. Action requires minds. Morality is mind-dependent. Full stop.

This is what both debaters in the [Noerr vs Terry X debate](../../debates/noerr-teryx-subjective-objective-morality.md) couldn't see: accepting "objective = mind-independent" makes morality impossible by definition, because you've defined your terms so that norms about minded behavior can't qualify.

### Question 2: Is morality constitutive or elective?

**This is the real debate.** And it's productive because the terminology doesn't pre-answer it.

**The constitutive case** (the vault's position):
1. Morality is constitutive of agency — you can't be an agent without being in moral relationships
2. The is-ought bridge is crossed by [performative grounding](./is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md): acting presupposes norms; denying them while acting is self-undermining
3. The content of morality is constrained by reality: betrayal isolates, cooperation compounds, societies that violate core norms collapse
4. Independent civilizations converge on core norms — the diagnostic test from [relational objectivity](../epistemology/relational-objectivity.md)
5. You can be *wrong* about moral facts — there is a fact of the matter independent of any individual's opinion

**The elective case** (the opposition):
1. Morality is a human invention, a social technology, useful but not structurally necessary
2. Agents could function with different moral systems or no moral system
3. Convergence is explained by shared biology and game-theoretic pressures, not by moral facts
4. "You can be wrong" just means "your strategy is suboptimal," not that there's a moral fact

Both sides can use the constitutive/elective vocabulary without begging the question. The debate becomes substantive rather than definitional.

## How This Resolves Open Problem #5

The vault's [Open Problems](./open-problems.md) flagged the objective/subjective paradox:

> Path A: Morality requires agency → agency requires minds → morality is subjective
> Path B: Morality requires agency → given agency it's stance-independent → morality is objective
> Both use valid reasoning from the same premises.

**Resolution:** The paradox dissolves because "objective" and "subjective" are each doing double duty. Split them:

- Path A is correct on axis 1: morality IS mind-dependent
- Path B is correct on axis 2: morality IS constitutive (not arbitrary)
- There's no paradox. The two paths are answering different questions.

The open problem noted: "the dichotomy itself may be malformed when applied to agent-dependent domains." Correct — it IS malformed, and the constitutive/elective distinction is the repair.

## The Moral Argument for God — Revisited

The classical moral argument:
1. Objective morality exists
2. Objective means mind-independent
3. But morality requires minds
4. Therefore morality needs a mind that IS the ground — a universal mind
5. Therefore God exists

**What the constitutive/elective reframe does to this:**

Step 2 is the error. "Objective" conflates two axes. Replace it:

1. Morality is constitutive of agency (not arbitrary, constrained by reality)
2. Morality is mind-dependent (requires agents)
3. The constitutive relationship needs a source — either a universal mind (God) or the structure of agency itself
4. [Performative grounding](./is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md) provides a source that doesn't require a universal mind — agency itself generates the norms
5. A universal mind remains one possible explanation, but is no longer the *only* possible explanation

The argument doesn't disprove God. It downgrades the moral argument from a proof to a hypothesis — one explanation among at least two for why morality is constitutive of agency.

## The Vacuity Test — Killing the Mind-Independence Move

Once you've split the axes, there's a sharp test that finishes the mind-independence version of moral realism: **does the claim quantify over a coherent domain?**

Consider: *"stealing is wrong, as a mind-independent fact."*

Strip the minds out of the world. No stealers, no property owners, no acts of stealing. What does "stealing is wrong" refer to? Nothing. It's vacuously true in the same way "dragons should rule the 7 kingdoms" is vacuously true — a claim about a state that doesn't exist, with nothing to make it right or wrong.

If the foundationalist wants to keep the mind-independence version, they need to either show that the claim picks out something real in a mindless world, or they need to retreat. There are three retreat moves, and all three collapse back onto your framework:

### Move 1: "The moral fact exists in the mind of God — a necessary mind, so there's always a mind"

Fair move — but this *concedes* the ontology axis. The claim is no longer mind-independent; it's dependent on one specific necessary mind. The foundationalist has agreed with you that morality requires minds and narrowed the claim to a particular divine mind. That's a theological position about the *source* of the constitutive relationship, not a defense of mind-independent moral facts. They lose the realism-as-posed debate while possibly winning a different one.

### Move 2: "Wrongness is a Platonic form — it exists in abstract space, independent of instantiation"

This is the dragons move exactly. Postulate a fact about an abstract domain with no bridge to the concrete one. Then Euthyphro hits: if the form makes stealing wrong, then *the form* grounds morality, not God — God is just a very good form-reader. If God makes the form wrong-flavored, then the form doesn't ground anything, God does, and we're back in Move 1.

### Move 3: "Stealing *would be* wrong if agents and property existed"

The dispositional / conditional move. It's true — but it's exactly the constitutive claim. *Given* the existence of agents and property, the norm follows from the structure of agent-interaction. That's not mind-independence realism; that's constitutive realism. The foundationalist has walked into your framework without noticing.

### What survives the vacuity test

The constitutive claim does: *"for any world containing agents with property and capacity for action, the norm against stealing is generated by the structure of agent-interaction."*

This isn't a claim about an impossible state — it's a claim about what necessarily follows once the conditions hold. Same logical structure as *"in any system with Peano arithmetic, 2+2=4."* The arithmetic fact isn't floating in Platonic ether; it's entailed by the axioms. The moral fact isn't floating either; it's entailed by the structure of agency.

### The payoff

The foundationalist's *actual* live position, stripped of the mind-independence claim that fails the vacuity test, is not about realism at all. It's about whether agency was **designed** to have this structure or **emerged** with it. That's a much more interesting question — and it's empirically inert from inside the structure (you can't tell designed from emergent by examining the product).

Which means the traditional realism debate has been a 2,400-year detour. Once you kill mind-independence with the vacuity test, the only live question is the source of the constitutive relationship: performative grounding, game-theoretic selection, or divine design. All three operate on mind-*dependent* constitutive facts. The debate has been asking the wrong question.

## Connections to Existing Framework

### Logic and Mathematics

The [logic series](../logic-and-math/README.md) already holds: "The patterns they describe are real. The formalisms themselves are constructions."

Same structure. Logic is mind-dependent (formal systems require minds to construct and use) but constitutive (you can't reason without presupposing logical norms). Nobody calls logic "subjective" — but by the strict "objective = mind-independent" definition, it would be. The constitutive/elective distinction explains why this feels wrong: logic is mind-dependent but constitutive, so calling it subjective conflates the axes.

### The Scope Model

The [scope model](./scope-confusion.md) maps cleanly onto the constitutive/elective gradient:

| Scope | Constitutive or elective? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Core norms** | Strongly constitutive — no functioning agent-society can violate these | Don't murder, don't steal |
| **Medium norms** | Constitutive but with more variation in implementation | Nurture children, honor agreements |
| **Marginal norms** | Gradient toward elective | Dietary rules, sexual norms, aesthetic standards |
| **Preferences** | Fully elective | Ice cream, music, fashion |

The constitutive/elective axis IS the scope gradient, just named. The scope model was already tracking this — the new terminology makes it explicit.

### Relational Objectivity

The [four-category ontology](../epistemology/relational-objectivity.md) provides the metaphysical infrastructure. The constitutive/elective distinction provides the *vocabulary* for communicating it:

- Category 3 (agent-relative) = mind-dependent + constitutive
- Category 4 (mind-dependent) = mind-dependent + elective
- The diagnostic tests (convergence, error, causal consequence) are tests for *constitutiveness*

## Open Questions

1. **Is the constitutive/elective boundary sharp or gradient?** The scope model suggests gradient. But is there a principled threshold, or just a continuous fade?
2. **Can something shift from elective to constitutive?** Nutrition history suggests yes — "eat what tastes good" was elective until we discovered vitamins, at which point "eat vitamin C" became constitutive. Are there moral analogues?
3. **Does the theist have a stronger version?** "Constitutive of agency" explains morality's normative force. But "constitutive of reality as designed by God" would explain it AND explain why the structure of agency has this property. Is the naturalist explanation complete, or does it push the question back one level?
4. **Can you get someone to accept this reframe in a live debate?** The terminological shift requires the opponent to abandon "objective/subjective" and adopt new vocabulary mid-conversation. That's a high bar rhetorically, even if it's correct philosophically.
