## Grounding Morality

### "Where do morals come from without God?"

**The short answer:** From agency. To act is to value. To value is to be subject to moral evaluation. Agency and morality are coextensive — you can't have one without the other. God is unnecessary to this chain.

**The full argument:** [Performative grounding](./README.md). The person asking "why be moral?" has already answered the question — they're alive, acting, choosing to ask. Their behavior demonstrates a preference for continued existence that their words pretend to question. Denying your existence, consciousness, or agency requires using all three. See also: [Performative Grounding — Lineage](./performative-grounding-lineage.md) for 2,400 years of independent precedent (Aristotle, Apel, Gewirth, Hoppe, Korsgaard).

**If they push TAG:** "Only God can ground morality/logic/uniformity." Two independent demolitions: (1) TAG is question-begging — "only God provides a universal ground" IS the conclusion restated as a premise; (2) the vault provides an alternative ground (agency), so "only God" is factually false. See [bridge planning](../../../notes/bridge-logic-to-morality.md) for the full two-vector attack.

### "Name one absolute moral truth"

**The trap:** "Murder is wrong" begs the question (murder = wrongful killing by definition). "Stealing is wrong" invites edge cases. Any specific rule can be challenged with a sufficiently exotic scenario.

**The response:** The framework doesn't need absolutes. It needs probabilistic heuristics validated by aggregate outcomes — the same way engineering doesn't need absolute certainty about material properties to build bridges that stand. "Don't murder" has been tested across every known civilization and environment. That's not an absolute — it's a convergent result so robust that demanding a stronger standard is demanding more of morality than we demand of physics.

**The deeper move:** Ask them to name an absolute moral truth from *their* framework. "God says so" pushes the question to the deity (Euthyphro). "It's self-evident" is the axiomatic stop they're criticizing you for not having. Every framework bottoms out somewhere — the question is whether the bottom is productive, not whether it's absolute.

### "That's just relativism"

**It's not.** Relativism says all moral positions are equally valid. The framework says moral positions are empirically testable — some produce flourishing, others produce collapse. The data is objective even if the measurements are imperfect. "Different cultures have different moral codes" is an observation, not a conclusion. Different cultures also had different medical practices — that doesn't mean medicine is relative.

**The relational objectivity move:** See [Relational Objectivity](../epistemology/relational-objectivity.md). Moral facts are relational (they depend on the existence of agents) without being subjective (they're not a matter of opinion). "Water boils at 100°C at sea level" is relational (depends on pressure) without being subjective. Same structure.

## Deontology, Consequentialism, and "I Just Follow God"

### "I don't care about outcomes, I only follow God's will"

**The observation:** Even within the tradition that claims purest deontology, the exemplars don't follow blindly:

- **Job** — questioned God directly, demanded an accounting for his suffering. God didn't punish the questioning — He answered it. The tradition models *wrestling with God*, not blind submission.
- **Christ at Gethsemane** — "My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me" (Matthew 26:39). This is weighing outcomes — experiencing doubt and pain — before choosing submission. If even Christ had a consequentialist moment before choosing the deontological path, "I just follow" understates what's actually happening.
- **Abraham and Isaac** — the entire point of the test is that it's *hard*. Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac is meaningful precisely because he understood and weighed the consequences. Blind obedience without understanding wouldn't be faith — it would be indifference.

**The authority chain:** The "I just follow" claim extends beyond God to practical life:
- Spouse → "honor thy husband/wife"
- Child → "honor thy father and mother"
- Employee → follow the boss
- Citizen → obey the law / serve the nation

But in every case, people evaluate outcomes constantly. The soldier who refuses an unlawful order, the employee who quits a toxic job, the spouse who leaves an abusive marriage — all consequentialist breaks in the deontological chain. Nobody actually follows blindly at every level. The question isn't whether you evaluate outcomes — you do. The question is whether you admit it.

**The vault's framing:** [Deontology is cached consequentialism](./deontology-consequentialism.md). Following God's rules isn't the opposite of caring about outcomes — the rules exist BECAUSE they produce good outcomes. "Don't steal" survived 3,000+ years because societies that prohibit theft outperform those that don't. The rule IS the consequence, compressed into a heuristic.

## Action, Inaction, and Rights

### "If you see a drowning child and do nothing, aren't you immoral?"

**The structural answer:** [Action/inaction asymmetry](./README.md#the-actioninaction-asymmetry). At any moment you perform one action; the things you're not doing are infinite. You can't be equally accountable for the infinite complement. Not rescuing one drowning child is morally identical in *structure* (though not in emotional weight) to not rescuing every other person in danger on the planet at that moment — all of which you're also not doing.

**The nuance:** Deliberate inaction in the face of a specific, identifiable obligation you agreed to IS evaluable — a lifeguard who watches someone drown has a contracted duty. But the obligation comes from the prior *action* of accepting the role, not from a general duty to act that applies to everyone near water.

**The emotional vs structural distinction:** Most people feel that walking past a drowning child is monstrous. The framework doesn't disagree — it says this feeling is a useful moral heuristic (and one that convergent traditions endorse). But "I feel this is wrong" is not the same as "you have a legal obligation enforceable by the state." The scope confusion distinction: morality-scope (you should help) vs law-scope (we can force you to help) are different questions.

### "What about positive rights — healthcare, education, food?"

**The framework's answer:** Negative rights (don't aggress) require refraining from a specific action — feasible and evaluable. Positive rights (you must provide X) require selecting specific actions from the infinite set of things you're not doing, which reintroduces the [weighting problem](../epistemology/weighting-problem.md): who decides which positive duty, how much, enforced by whom?

**The practical problem:** Every positive right is a claim on someone else's labor or resources. "You have a right to healthcare" means "someone else is obligated to provide it." Who? How much? At what cost to their other obligations? These aren't rhetorical questions — they're the weighting problem in action, and they have no objective answer.

**What the framework supports instead:** Voluntary provision through markets, charity, mutual aid, and community. These work through the morality-scope (social pressure, reputation, reciprocity) rather than law-scope (state compulsion). The [scope confusion](./scope-confusion.md) insight: most positive-rights arguments are scope errors — confusing "society should help the sick" (true, morality-scope) with "the state must force everyone to fund healthcare" (scope escalation).

## The Sheriff Problem and Utilitarian Traps

### "Wouldn't you sacrifice one to save five?"

**The vault's four independent arguments against:** See [The Sheriff Problem](../../debates/utilitarianism-sheriff-counterexample.md) for the full treatment. In brief:

1. **Computational irreducibility** — you can't actually compute the consequences. The "simple" 1-vs-5 calculation ignores cascading second- and third-order effects.
2. **The weighting problem** — "greatest good for the greatest number" requires an aggregation function that is itself subjective.
3. **Action/inaction asymmetry** — killing one is an action (singular, evaluable). Not saving five is part of the infinite complement.
4. **Scope confusion** — the scenario smuggles in law-scope enforcement (you MUST sacrifice one) for what is actually a morality-scope question (should you?).

## Meta-Objections

### "Your framework is just libertarianism dressed up as philosophy"

**Partially fair.** The framework reaches libertarian-compatible conclusions on many questions. But it reaches them from different grounds than most libertarians use: not from natural rights axioms (Rothbard), not from argumentation ethics (Hoppe), not from utility maximization (Friedman), but from agency + empirical observation + scope analysis. When the framework agrees with libertarianism, it's because the data converges there — not because the conclusion was assumed.

Where the framework *disagrees* with standard libertarianism: it acknowledges that pure voluntary order may be insufficient (the [Gödel Governance Problem](../the-godel-governance-problem.md)), that some government functions may be necessary evils, and that the scope model allows for law-scope enforcement where performative grounding is strongest.

### "You can't derive ought from is"

**Correct — and we don't.** See [the is-ought resolution](./README.md). We don't derive ought from is through logic. The "is" already contains the "ought" because agents who exist are already acting, and acting presupposes valuation. Hume was looking for a logical bridge when the questioner was already standing on the other side.
