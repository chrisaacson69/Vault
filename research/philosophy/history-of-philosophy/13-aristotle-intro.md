# Episode 13: Aristotle — A Philosopher for Living on Earth
> Aristotle rejects both Sophistic subjectivism and Platonic supernaturalism, establishing the third alternative: one reality, this one, knowable through senses and reason — making him "the philosopher" and the father of the primacy of existence.

**Source:** [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qvl4f8l7Ctk) (41:11)
**Links:** [Series Index](./README.md), [Episode 12: Plato's Politics](./12-plato-politics.md)

## The Argument

### Part I: The Two Alternatives So Far

Peikoff opens by recapping the state of play in the history of philosophy. Two fundamental approaches have been on offer:

**The Heraclitean/Sophist line:**
- Metaphysics: no objective reality; nothing *is*, everything is *becoming*
- Epistemology: knowledge is impossible; man is the measure of all things; all is opinion
- Ethics: whim-worshipping subjectivism; might makes right

**The Pythagorean/Platonic line:**
- Metaphysics: two realities — a superior, non-material world of Forms and this imperfect reflection
- Epistemology: objective knowledge exists but is gained by contact with the supernatural dimension (recollection, mystic insight)
- Ethics: objective, but consists of turning away from life on earth — "practicing the profession of dying" — and ruling men by force

In short: no reality vs. two realities; skepticism vs. mysticism; whim-worship vs. ascetic supernaturalism. Both lead to tyranny (as episode 12 demonstrated).

### Part II: Aristotle's Stature

Peikoff introduces Aristotle with comparable praise to Plato — original, profound, with a brilliant capacity for systematic integration — but adds a "crucial difference": Aristotle's philosophy, *in its essentials, is true.* This makes him "a phenomenon without precedent in the history of thought."

**Historical markers:**
- Dante called him "the master of them that know" — "a simple statement of the exact truth"
- In the medieval period he was referred to simply as "the philosopher"
- Peikoff's claim: "If truth on essential fundamental issues is a vital part of philosophy, then Aristotle's is the only philosophy... Aristotelianism is philosophy as a rational science, versus philosophy as a rationalization for subjective whims or mystic trances"

**Ayn Rand's thesis:** The history of the West has been "a duel between Plato and Aristotle." Whenever Platonism was dominant: mysticism, regression, brutality, suffering. Whenever Aristotelianism was dominant: reason, progress, freedom, human happiness. Every major human achievement — modern science, the Industrial Revolution, the founding of America — was built on Aristotle's foundations.

### Part III: Aristotle's Life and the State of His Writings

**Biographical sketch:**
- Born 384 BC in Stagira, Thrace (hence "the Stagirite")
- Entered Plato's Academy at 18, studied there ~20 years until Plato's death
- For most of those years, was a wholehearted Platonist — believed in Forms, immortality of the soul, the wheel of birth, knowledge as reminiscence
- Gradually questioned and rejected Plato's views, developing his own philosophy in fundamental opposition
- Opened the Lyceum in 335 BC in direct competition with Plato's Academy; taught while strolling in a covered walkway (*peripatos*), hence "the Peripatetic philosopher"
- Wrote an incredible number of treatises covering physics, metaphysics, logic, epistemology, ethics, psychology, biology, rhetoric, theology, politics, aesthetics — "one of the very few universal geniuses in human history"

**The textual problem:** Almost all works Aristotle wrote for the general public are lost. What survives are likely lecture notes (his own or his students'), terse, telegraphic, highly technical, with later additions pasted into wrong locations. Elements of early Platonism pop up randomly in mature works. "Do not blame yourself if you find Aristotle difficult to untangle — it is not your fault and it is not his fault."

### Part IV: The Attack on Plato's World of Forms

Aristotle's works contain a "repeated polemic" against the Forms. Peikoff presents several arguments:

**1. The Forms are useless.** This world — the one we live in, the one we need to understand — is a world of particular things that move, change, and develop. How does reference to another world of static, motionless universals explain any of that? It doesn't.

**2. The Forms are a useless duplication.** Down here we have shoes, ships, and cabbages. Plato's idea of making sense of this is to posit *another* world of shoe-ness, ship-ness, and cabbage-ness. "That is senseless," says Aristotle.

**3. "Participation" is unintelligible.** Plato says this world "reflects" or "imitates" or "participates in" the Forms, but what does that actually *mean*? All he offers are empty metaphors. In fact, you have two sundered worlds with no real connection between them.

**4. The Third Man argument.** Plato's principle: whenever two or more similar things exist, their common denominator exists separately as a Form they all reflect. But do Socrates and the Form of Man have something in common? Obviously yes (both are called "man"). Then by Plato's own principle, there must be a *third* entity — a further Form that Socrates and the Form of Man both reflect. And so on to infinity. (Plato himself raised this objection in the *Parmenides* and admitted he had no answer. Aristotle simply adds: "You have no answer because your theory is hopeless.")

**5. The fundamental objection: self-contradiction.** Plato makes the universal into a concrete, individual, particular thing — and this *obliterates* the distinction between universal and particular. A particular is a self-contained, self-enclosed *this* that exists in itself. A universal, by definition, is what is common to many particulars — it can't exist in itself, only in other things. To say the universal is a separate entity existing on its own is to make it a particular, i.e., a non-universal. Direct self-contradiction. Plato's error: **confusing abstractions with entities** — the *fallacy of reification* (literally "thing-making").

### Part V: Aristotle's Own Position on Universals

Aristotle's answer: universals are real, distinguishable from particulars (Plato was right about that), and they are the basis and objects of conceptual thought (Plato was right about that too). **But only particulars exist.** Universals exist *only in* particulars.

**"This-such."** Every existing thing is a metaphysical compound of two elements:
- **Form** (the universal element): the characteristics it shares with other things of the same kind — what makes it *a such* (a man, a horse, a shoe)
- **Matter** (the individualizing element): what makes it *a this* — a unique, unrepeatable particular

**Note:** Aristotle's "matter" is a specialized technical term. It does not mean physical matter in the modern sense. It means whatever makes a thing uniquely *this* thing rather than another of the same kind.

**The Aristotelian law:** No matter without form, no form without matter. Both previous schools violated this:
- Plato: form without matter (universals existing apart from any particulars)
- Heraclitus: matter without form (stuff in flux with no stable identity or nature)

This position is called **Aristotelian realism** (vs. Platonic realism): universals are real, but they exist only in the particulars that instantiate them.

### Part VI: The Categories — Entities as Primary

In the *Categories*, Aristotle classifies the fundamental types of existent and establishes: the **entity** (individual thing, "primary substance") is the fundamental constituent of reality.

- **Actions** are actions *of* entities. "You can't have a room filled with running unless there's something running."
- **Quantities** are quantities *of* entities. "You can't have a room filled with six — six *what?*"
- **Qualities** (loud, beautiful), **relations** (above, below, uncle of) — all derivative forms of existence. None can exist apart from entities.

**The upshot:** Reality is the world of common sense. "You are not a reflection or a flux or a contradiction or a dream or a nothing or a string of essences polluted by empty space. Reality is this world as it appears to human senses."

### Part VII: Primacy of Existence vs. Primacy of Consciousness

Peikoff identifies the deepest principle implicit in Aristotle's metaphysics — one Aristotle himself did not explicitly formulate but which his philosophy embodies:

**Primacy of existence:** Reality comes first. It is the metaphysical primary. Reality is what it is independent of the content or actions of any consciousness. Consciousness is the faculty of perceiving, grasping, and identifying the facts of reality. It cannot alter those facts — no amount of wishing, hoping, fearing, or believing can magically change what is.

**Primacy of consciousness (the opposite):** Consciousness comes first. It is the metaphysical primary. Reality is an offshoot or byproduct of consciousness; facts are whatever the ruling consciousness chooses them to be.

**Examples of the primacy of consciousness:**
- The Sophists: man's arbitrary feelings and opinions are the measure of all things. Your consciousness shapes reality for you. (Individual-level primacy of consciousness.)
- Plato: (a) The Forms are actually abstractions — phenomena pertaining to man's method of organizing sensory data — erected into separate entities that shape and control this world. Making the physical world a reflection of phenomena of consciousness. (b) The Demiurge myth: a soul/consciousness wandered by, desired order, and shaped the physical world accordingly. (c) Plato's very starting point: "Man must know; what must reality be to satisfy man's need for knowledge?" — starting from consciousness and deriving what reality must be. All are the primacy of consciousness by implication.
- Everyday evasion: "If I don't see it, it won't exist" — the implicit premise that consciousness controls reality.

**Aristotle's approach (the exact opposite):** Don't ask "what must reality be to satisfy our knowledge?" Ask "what, as a matter of fact, *is* reality?" Then: given reality's nature, by what processes can we acquire knowledge of it? First comes reality; then, on the basis of its nature, epistemology.

**Aristotle vs. the pre-Socratics on facts:** When confronted with theories denying obvious facts (Zeno's arguments that you can't walk across a room), Aristotle's "characteristic attitude" is to conscientiously present the arguments, then say: "But we see these things. They are obvious. They are facts. And facts are facts." Then he proceeds to demolish the arguments that led to denying the facts. He is "pre-eminently the realist in philosophy."

**Caveat:** Aristotle is not a fully consistent primacy-of-existence thinker. There is always "a vestigial contradictory Platonic element in him to the very end of his days." Peikoff will note these touches throughout the series but considers them unimportant to Aristotle *qua Aristotelian*.

## Analysis

### What Works

**The framing of two dead-ends converging on a third alternative is superb pedagogy.** By the time Peikoff gets to Aristotle, you *want* the third option. Twelve episodes of watching subjectivism and mysticism each fail in their own way — and then converge on tyranny — creates intellectual demand for what Aristotle offers. This is Peikoff at his best as a teacher: structuring the narrative so that Aristotle's positions feel not like arbitrary assertions but like the only moves left on the board.

**The attack on Plato's Forms is devastating and cumulatively powerful.** Any single argument (uselessness, duplication, unintelligible participation, the Third Man, reification) might be parried by a clever Platonist. Together, they constitute a comprehensive assault that leaves the theory with nowhere to go. The reification argument in particular — that making a universal into a separate entity makes it a particular, thereby contradicting the universal/particular distinction — is genuinely philosophically deep. It's not just "wrong" but *self-defeating* on its own terms.

**The "this-such" formulation is elegant and clarifying.** Every entity is both unique (*this*) and classifiable (*such*). The universal and particular are not two worlds but two aspects of every existing thing. This dissolves Plato's problem without Plato's costs — you keep the reality of universals (they ground conceptual knowledge) without the supernatural duplication. It's the kind of solution that, once stated, seems obvious — which is the hallmark of a good philosophical move.

**The primacy of existence/consciousness distinction is the most consequential framework in the episode.** It cuts deeper than any particular metaphysical claim. It identifies a *methodological* divide: do you start from reality and ask how consciousness can know it, or start from consciousness and ask what reality must be to accommodate it? Nearly every philosophical error Peikoff has catalogued in the series reduces to some version of the primacy of consciousness — and Aristotle's primacy of existence provides the corrective. The everyday evasion example (bedroom scene) makes the abstract principle concrete and recognizable.

### What to Watch

**"Aristotle's philosophy in its essentials is true" is stated, not argued.** Peikoff presents this as a straightforward claim, and it functions as the framing for the entire Aristotle section of the course. But it front-loads the evaluation — the listener is told the verdict before hearing the evidence. This is pedagogically effective (it signals what to pay attention to) but philosophically risky. A student who accepts this framing may fail to evaluate Aristotle's specific arguments critically, treating difficulties as mere "vestigial Platonic elements" rather than genuine problems. The disclaimer about Platonic residues in Aristotle's thought is appropriate, but the overall tone is closer to advocacy than analysis.

**The "Plato vs. Aristotle" civilizational narrative is too clean.** Rand's thesis — Platonism dominant = dark ages, Aristotelianism dominant = progress — is a powerful heuristic but compresses enormously complex history into a binary. The Islamic Golden Age preserved and extended Aristotle while producing neither freedom nor individualism in the modern Western sense. The Scientific Revolution drew on both Platonic (mathematical idealism) and Aristotelian (empirical observation) strands. The American founding was influenced by Locke, who was an empiricist (Aristotelian lineage) but also by natural law theory (heavily Thomistic, which is Aristotle filtered through Plato-influenced Christianity). The real history is a tangle, not a duel.

**Aristotle's own position on universals has an unaddressed tension.** Universals exist "only in particulars" — but what happens when all particulars of a kind are destroyed? Does the universal cease to exist? If the last dodo dies, does "dodo-ness" still exist? If not, then universals are contingent on the existence of their instances, which makes them less fundamental than Plato claimed. If yes, then they're not really "only in" particulars after all. Aristotle (and Peikoff here) doesn't address this directly, and it became one of the major points of contention in medieval philosophy (the problem of universals in the Porphyrian tradition).

**The primacy of consciousness critique of Plato is strong but somewhat anachronistic.** Peikoff reads Plato's starting point ("knowledge must be real; what must reality be to accommodate it?") as implicitly asserting the primacy of consciousness. But this reading imports an Objectivist framework that Plato wouldn't have recognized. Plato thought he *was* being realistic — the Forms are the *most* real things, not products of consciousness. The charge that Forms are "actually abstractions" reified into entities is Aristotle's interpretation, which Plato would have rejected. Peikoff is right that Plato's method has the *structure* of consciousness-primacy, but stating it as though it's a simple fact rather than a controversial interpretation obscures the philosophical distance between the claim and its target.

### History's Verdict

**On Aristotle's rejection of separate Forms:** Largely upheld. No subsequent mainstream philosophy has returned to Plato's literal theory of separate, self-subsisting universals. Medieval realism (Aquinas) followed Aristotle in placing universals in things. Modern philosophy has debated the status of universals extensively (nominalism, conceptualism, trope theory, structural realism), but virtually no one defends the full Platonic picture. Aristotle's core objection — that you can't make a universal into a particular without contradiction — has proven decisive.

**On the entity as primary substance:** Enormously influential but contested. Aristotle's insistence that individual things (not processes, not quantities, not relations) are the fundamental furniture of reality shaped Western ontology for two millennia. Modern physics has complicated this picture: quantum field theory describes reality in terms of fields and excitations rather than Aristotelian "primary substances." Process philosophy (Whitehead) explicitly challenges the entity-primacy assumption. But in ordinary life and in the special sciences (biology, chemistry, even most of physics), we still reason about entities with properties — Aristotle's framework remains the default.

**On the primacy of existence:** This principle has become more, not less, relevant. Post-Kantian philosophy increasingly subordinated reality to the structures of consciousness (Hegel's absolute idealism, phenomenology, social constructionism, postmodernism). Each of these movements, in Peikoff's framework, represents a new version of the primacy of consciousness. The counter-movement — scientific realism, naturalism, the common-sense tradition — implicitly endorses Aristotle's principle. The debate is very much alive: the question of whether reality is independent of our conceptual schemes, or constituted by them, remains one of the central disputes in contemporary philosophy.

**On Aristotle as "the philosopher":** The assessment has fluctuated dramatically. He was the undisputed intellectual authority from the 13th through the 16th century, was violently rejected during the Scientific Revolution (when his physics proved wrong), and has experienced a revival since the mid-20th century in ethics (virtue ethics), metaphysics (neo-Aristotelianism in analytic philosophy), and philosophy of biology. The current consensus: his specific scientific claims are often wrong, but his philosophical *method* — starting from observation, demanding that theories conform to facts, refusing to sacrifice the evident for the theoretical — has aged remarkably well.
