## The Argument

### The Pythagorean School

Pythagoras (fl. ~530 BC) can't be separated from his followers — no documents survive to distinguish his views from theirs. The school endured through the end of pagan philosophy. They lived communally without private property, functioning as a religious brotherhood. Peikoff: "far and away the most otherworldly, the most mystic of all the pre-Socratics... the first religious philosophers that we encounter in this course."

Key claim: **neither Plato nor Christianity would have been possible without the Pythagoreans.**

### The Orphic Mystery Religion

The Pythagoreans didn't follow the Olympian gods. They subscribed to **Orphism** — an oriental mystery cult imported into Greece, a minority position regarded by most Greeks as a lunatic fringe. This is the religion Peikoff flagged in the Thales lecture as the exception to the Greek norm.

Core Orphic doctrines:
- **Soul/body dualism** — man has two parts in eternal conflict. The soul is the high part, akin to God; the body is low. The body is the **prison** or **tomb** of the soul.
- **The Fall** — the soul was once a god-like creature in a superior spiritual world, but sinned and fell. Imprisonment in a body on earth is the punishment.
- **Reincarnation** — the "wheel of birth." At death the soul returns to the other world for reward/punishment, then comes back in a new body (human or animal). Pythagoras allegedly recognized a friend's soul in a beaten dog.
- **Purification** — the goal is to escape the body permanently and reunite with God. This requires ascetic, pleasure-denying life plus Orphic ritual. (Peikoff: "the Pythagoreans at their most ascetic are frenzied hedonists compared to the Christians yet to come.")
- **Suicide is forbidden** — you belong to God; it's up to him to decide when you go home. "God's property rights."

The Orphic origin myth of Dionysus: born from Zeus, slain and devoured by the evil Titans, reborn from Zeus's thigh. Zeus destroyed the Titans and formed man from their ashes — so man is a dual creature, part evil Titan substance, part divine Dionysian substance. Salvation = freeing the divine within from the bondage of the body.

Peikoff's verdict: **"This is the primary source of the soul/body opposition in Western civilization. No better argument for that opposition has ever been put forth."** He connects it directly to modern sexual guilt, anti-materialism, and hostility toward capitalism.

### The Scientific Side: All Things Are Numbers

The Pythagoreans asked the same question as every pre-Socratic: what is the world stuff? Their special interest — mathematics — shaped their answer.

**The discoveries:** They found mathematics *everywhere*:
- Musical harmony is based on mathematical ratios (string lengths)
- Musical relationships can be expressed numerically (intervals: fifth, fourth, octave)
- Mathematics governs astronomy (first hints of mathematical celestial laws)
- Health consists of mathematical balance of bodily elements (the right ratio = health; imbalance = disease)

**The leap:** Just as Thales seized on water and Heraclitus on change, the Pythagoreans seized on numbers and made them metaphysical: **"All things are numbers."** Numbers are the world stuff.

**The confusion:** What could this mean? How can things *be* numbers? They represented numbers with physical pebbles and dots, confusing numbers with the physical objects symbolizing them. This was like a primitive atomic theory. And they were genuine numerological mystics: justice = 4 (or 9), marriage = 5, love = 8, man = 250, plants = 360.

Peikoff's comment on the 13th-floor skyscraper: the moderns are worse than the Pythagoreans — "if the Pythagoreans thought 13 was bad luck, they would stop the building on the 12th floor. They wouldn't add subjectivism — they didn't call 13 '14'."

**The real insight buried in the mysticism:** Mathematics is vital for discovering the laws of the world. Modern physics is impossible without mathematically formulated laws. Kepler, a devout Pythagorean, searched for years for mathematical laws of planetary motion precisely because he believed "all things are numbers" — and found them. Modern science is partly a development of this Pythagorean discovery, though it didn't bear fruit until the Renaissance.

### Two Worlds: The Attempted Solution

By the 4th century BC, the later Pythagoreans realized numbers can't literally *be* the ingredients of things. They shifted to: numbers or numerical relations **govern** the behavior of things. But they took "govern" literally — as if a disembodied law in another dimension commands things to obey, like a king commanding subjects.

This produces **metaphysical dualism** — two realities:

| World of Numbers | This World |
|---|---|
| Grasped by reason | Grasped by the senses |
| Unchanging, immutable (2+2=4 forever) | Constantly changing |
| True reality | Derivative, inferior |

**The claimed solution to Heraclitus vs. Parmenides:** Give each philosopher his own world. Heraclitus said reality must be changing? Fine — this world is changing. Parmenides said true reality must be unchanging? Fine — the world of numbers is unchanging. Problem solved (or so they thought).

**Plato picked this up** and transformed it into his Theory of Forms.

### Three Types of Men (Olympic Games Hierarchy)

The Pythagoreans distinguished three types of people at the Olympic Games, ranked from lowest to highest:

1. **Lovers of gain** — those who come to buy and sell (popcorn vendors). Most attached to the physical.
2. **Athletes** — motivated by honor, fame, glory. Still materialistic but less crude.
3. **Spectators** — those who simply want to observe and understand. Disinterested love of knowledge. Most detached from the physical.

The spectators — the philosophers — are the highest type, but only if their knowledge is *disinterested*: non-commercial, non-practical, divorced from life on earth. Philosophy and science are preached as **religious rites of purification**, not tools for living.

Peikoff: "This is the earliest severing in Western philosophy of knowledge from life." Plato will develop this into a full psychology and, ultimately, the case for philosopher-kings ruling a communist dictatorship.

### Epistemological Legacy

The Pythagoreans bequeathed the view that **only mathematics qualifies as true knowledge**. Peikoff identifies the modern descendant: the type who won't accept a claim as "scientific" unless it has numbers in it. "Human beings need self-esteem" is vague and unscientific; "they need 3.9 units of self-esteem" is rigorous. Pure Pythagorean number fixation.

## Analysis

### What Works

**The Orphic connection is the episode's real payload.** This is the lecture where you see the body/soul dichotomy enter Western philosophy with a specific origin story and a specific transmission path. It's not a timeless human intuition — it's a particular doctrine from a particular mystery cult, imported into philosophy by a particular school, handed to Plato, and through him to Christianity. Being able to trace this genealogy is genuinely powerful.

**The package-deal insight.** Peikoff's observation that the Pythagoreans' legitimate scientific discoveries got propagated *together* with their Orphic mysticism is one of the most important structural points in the course. This is how bad ideas survive: they attach to good ones. Mathematics is real; numerological mysticism is not. But they traveled as a unit, and Plato inherited both.

**The two-world "solution" to Heraclitus/Parmenides.** Showing that metaphysical dualism arose as a specific *attempted answer* to a specific *problem* makes it intelligible rather than arbitrary. The Pythagoreans didn't just invent two worlds for fun — they were trying to reconcile two arguments that seemed irrefutable. The solution is wrong, but it's not crazy.

### What to Watch

**The soul/body origin claim is too strong.** "This is the primary source of the soul/body opposition in Western civilization" — this is only true for the *Western philosophical tradition*. Soul/body dualism exists independently in Hinduism (atman/body, predating the Pythagoreans by centuries), Zoroastrianism, and various shamanic traditions. The Orphic version is the specific one that entered Greek philosophy and then Christianity, but calling it "the primary source" ignores the broader human pattern. The question of *why* humans across cultures independently develop body/soul dualisms — perhaps rooted in the phenomenology of dreaming, out-of-body experiences, or death — goes unasked.

**The modern parallels are fun but loose.** Connecting Orphism to modern sexual guilt and anti-capitalism is vivid but skips a lot of steps. The Victorian sexual repression Peikoff implies has many causes beyond Orphic→Platonic→Christian transmission — including economic factors, disease anxiety, and social control mechanisms. The causal chain is real but not as direct as the lecture suggests.

**"Knowledge divorced from life" as criticism.** Peikoff presents disinterested knowledge as obviously wrong — a severing of theory from practice. But there's a real question about whether *all* knowledge must be instrumental. Pure mathematics, theoretical physics, and philosophy itself often advance precisely because someone pursued understanding without demanding immediate practical payoff. The Pythagorean error isn't disinterested inquiry per se — it's treating disinterest as a *spiritual* requirement and *denigrating* practical application.

### History's Verdict

**On "all things are numbers":** Stunningly vindicated in spirit, wrong in letter. Modern physics is mathematical to its core. The Standard Model, general relativity, quantum mechanics — all are mathematical structures that describe reality with extraordinary precision. The Pythagorean insight that mathematical relationships are fundamental to understanding nature is one of the most consequential ideas in human history. But things aren't *made of* numbers; they're *described by* mathematical relationships. The map/territory distinction the Pythagoreans lacked.

**On the soul/body split:** Still enormously influential, but increasingly without philosophical support. Neuroscience has progressively closed the gap between "mental" and "physical" phenomena. Descartes's dualism was the last major philosophical defense; since then, the trend has been toward various forms of physicalism or property dualism. The Orphic version — the soul as a divine prisoner in a material jail — has no scientific support and survives only as religious doctrine.

**On two-world metaphysics:** The most consequential legacy. Plato's Forms, the medieval heaven/earth split, Kant's phenomena/noumena, and even the modern distinction between mathematical models and physical reality all echo the Pythagorean structure. Whether reality has "levels" or is a single integrated system remains a live question. The Pythagoreans opened it; it's still not closed.

**On the hierarchy of men:** The ranking of contemplation above commerce has had massive cultural staying power — from Aristotle through the medieval contempt for trade to the modern academy's suspicion of "applied" work. Whether this reflects genuine insight about human flourishing or just the class interests of intellectuals is a question the vault's economics section would answer differently than Peikoff does.
