# Originalism and the Limiting Document
> Justices swear to support *this* Constitution — not what they think it says or feel it should do. Read it like a contract: fixed meaning at adoption, change it by amendment. The analogy holds for the clear text (most of the document) and breaks only in the deliberately-broad *construction zone* — and conflating the two is how the smuggling happens. The keystone the Wickard/Anderson/Chiafalo trilogy points up to.

## Start with the oath — it's where judicial review itself comes from

In *Marbury v. Madison* (1803), Marshall's *reason* that courts may strike down statutes is the judicial oath:

> "Why does a judge swear to discharge his duties agreeably to the constitution of the United States, if that constitution forms no rule for his government?... How immoral to impose [the oath] on them, if they were to be used as the instruments... for violating what they swear to support!"

So the power of judicial review **exists only because** the judge is bound to the text as law. That yields a lever sharper than the oath alone: **a judge who wields review to impose his preferences saws off the branch he sits on.** Review and constraint flow from the *same* source. You cannot keep the Marbury power while ditching the fidelity that is its only warrant.

And you can only swear allegiance to a **fixed object.** If "this Constitution" floats with each judge's era or feelings, the oath has no referent — it collapses into "I swear to support whatever I decide," an oath to *yourself*. So the raw-preference mode — *"I feel it should work this way"* — is not merely wrong; it is a **breach of a sworn, public precommitment.** The oath converts a methodological argument into an *integrity* argument, which is the right register for a judge who knows the text says X and rules Y anyway.

## Originalist, not just textualist

**Textualism** = read the words by their meaning. **Originalism** = read them with the meaning *fixed at the moment of adoption*. The oath and the contract analogy both depend on the *temporal anchor*: a pure textualist could drift to "what the words mean *now*"; the originalist says "what they meant *then* — and if you want them to mean something else, **amend**." Only originalism supplies the fixity the oath presupposes.

## The Constitution is a *limiting* document

This is the structural fact everything hangs on. The Constitution is not a grant of general power with carve-outs. It is the inverse:

- **Empowering parts** (Articles I–III): here is the government, here is *specifically* what it may do. **Default = no power** unless affirmatively granted (Art I §8 enumerates; the **10th Amendment** reserves the rest).
- **"Especially not" parts** (Bill of Rights): even where power might exist, *these* are walled off.

The two layers map onto a real founding fight: Hamilton (**Federalist 84**) argued *against* a Bill of Rights precisely because the government had only enumerated powers — "why declare that things shall not be done which there is no power to do?" — fearing a list of prohibitions would *imply* a general power the enumeration denied. The **9th Amendment** (enumeration of rights "shall not be construed to deny... others retained by the people") answers that worry. The 9th and 10th are the hinges of the limiting architecture.

**Why this is the deepest point:** for a limiting document, the burden runs on *the government* to show a power *exists* — not on the citizen to show a right exists. [Wickard](./wickard-v-filburn.md) **inverts** that: it presumes federal power and asks whether anything *stops* it (aggregation: nothing does). That is reading a limiting document as an *empowering* one — the structural betrayal beneath the textual one.

## Read it like a contract — and watch where the analogy breaks

Where there is an authoritative text, we read it as written, with the meaning it had when adopted. That's how we treat statutes and contracts; the Constitution is a text, so the burden is on the other side to justify an exception. (Caveat against over-claiming: "all law is textualist" overstates it — *torts* and much of contract/property are evolving **common law**, judge-made by design. The principle is narrower: *where there is a controlling text, original meaning governs.*)

The contract analogy is mostly right and underused. It breaks in four identifiable places — and only there:

1. **Consent (and a libertarian twist).** A contract binds its *signatories*; no living person signed the Constitution. Push the analogy hard and you get **Lysander Spooner** (*No Treason*, 1867): as a literal contract it's *void* for lack of consent. So part of *why* it isn't treated purely as a contract is that as a contract it's **defective** — it's classed as a charter/fundamental law instead. (The "compact among states" version also carries Calhoun/nullification baggage.)
2. **Standards, not rules — by design.** Contracts are usually specific and finite-term; the Constitution uses broad standards ("equal protection," "unreasonable," "cruel and unusual," "due process") meant to endure across unforeseeable facts. Even sophisticated originalists (Balkin's **framework originalism**) concede: *meaning* is fixed, *application* evolves — "unreasonable searches" reaches thermal imaging (*Kyllo*) and cell-site data (*Carpenter*). The founders built the construction zone in **on purpose**. The Constitution is special not in *kind* but in *degree*: a contract written almost entirely in open standards meant to outlive everyone who signed it.
3. **Amendment difficulty / the dead hand.** Contract parties renegotiate at will; the Constitution requires Article V supermajorities. Living constitutionalists say interpretation must do the adapting amendment can't. **Rebuttal:** the difficulty is a *feature* — entrenchment against passion — and "amendment is hard, so judges should amend by reading" is the exact **amend-don't-gut** violation. Fix amendment; don't license the cheat.
4. **Self-constitution.** A contract is enforced by an outside body; the Constitution *creates* that body. There is no higher court to appeal a constitutional reading to — the document defines the institution that defines the document. That's the **Gödel-governance** problem: no system can fully bind its own interpreters from within.

## Interpretation vs. construction — and where the sin lives

Lawrence Solum's distinction is the scalpel:

- **Interpretation** = recovering the text's linguistic meaning (what the words *meant*). Originalism is powerful here.
- **Construction** = giving legal effect where the text is *vague, ambiguous, or silent* — the **construction zone**, where the text runs out and judgment must enter from somewhere.

The legitimate job is judgment *in the genuine gap* (presidential power is the paradigm — Article II's terseness keeps war powers, removal, and privilege in permanent litigation). **The sin is "interpreting" the *clear*** — manufacturing ambiguity where none exists to reach a desired result. The construction zone is real and was built in on purpose; expanding it to swallow clear text is the corruption.

That yields the honest taxonomy:
- **Honest textualism** admits the construction zone and *disciplines* it (original public meaning, structural inference, liquidation-on-the-*correct*-practice, amend-don't-gut).
- **Fair-weather textualism** pretends the text decides everything, then smuggles the result into the gap while claiming the words compelled it — invoking text when it helps and tradition/consequences when it doesn't.

Proof that originalism isn't merely outcome-coded: ***Bostock v. Clayton County*** (2020) — Gorsuch rode textualism to a result the right hated. A method that sometimes bites your own side is a real method; one that always favors your side is partisanship with footnotes.

## Justice Robert Jackson — the through-character

One man embodies all three modes across the trilogy and its neighbors:
- **Wickard (1942)** — *faithless to clear text* (his worst moment; "commerce" → everything).
- **Ray v. Blair (1952), dissenting** — *faithful to clear text*: elector independence is "implicit in [the] text."
- **Youngstown (1952), concurring** — *disciplined judgment in the gap*: the three-zone framework for presidential power that remains the canonical method for the Article II construction zone.

Jackson is the proof the distinction isn't partisan — it's a craft you can do well or badly in the same career.

## The trilogy as three graded case studies

| Case | Where it lives | Failure | Direction / Reasoning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Wickard** | Interpretation | ignored *clear* meaning of "commerce" | wrong / unreasonable |
| **Anderson** | Construction zone | filled the §3-enforcement gap by *inventing* §5-exclusivity | **right** / unreasonable |
| **Chiafalo** | Construction zone | filled the elector-discretion gap by *liquidation on the wrong practice* | wrong / unreasonable |

Originalism's grip varies by case: decisive when text is clear (Wickard), weaker when text runs out (Anderson/Chiafalo) — which is *why* even sound method doesn't guarantee correct results.

## The honest counter (no strawman)

The living-constitutionalist's real rejoinder: *all* interpretation has a construction zone, so textualism's pretense of mechanical objectivity is *less* honest than openly reasoning about purpose — it just hides its value judgments. The reply: **a leaky constraint beats no constraint.** Text doesn't determine the answer in the gap, but it bounds the *range* of defensible answers; unconstrained purposivism lets the judge do anything. (This is the [Weighting Problem](./philosophy/epistemology/weighting-problem.md) in legal dress — the text underdetermines, and the gap-filling rule is the contested "aggregation function.")

## The ratchet — why this is a governance problem, not just a legal one

The Constitution is a **bundle of precommitment devices** engineered to resist power's natural accumulation: enumerated powers, the Bill of Rights, supermajority amendment, separation of powers as deliberate friction, and the oath. Every one is a brake.

But **Wickard + stare decisis = a one-way ratchet**: each expansion becomes precedent, reliance interests make reversal "too costly," and federal power clicks forward and almost never back (*Lopez*/*Morrison* are the rare reverse-clicks). This is [scope creep](./philosophy/morality/legal-theory/registration-and-scope-creep.md) at the constitutional meta-level: the document built to *stop* scope creep is itself subject to it, via interpretation.

And no document is self-enforcing (the [Gödel](./philosophy/dynamics/the-godel-governance-problem.md) point): the Constitution can't bind its own interpreters from within. It rests on a *culture of fidelity* (the oath taken seriously) and on [opposing forces](./philosophy/dynamics/opposing-forces.md) (branches, states, elections). When the culture erodes — judges who "interpret" the clear — the paper barriers fail, because the system needs humans to *choose* submission to the text. The structure can only make defection **costly, visible, and dishonorable**; it cannot make it impossible. That hands off to the parent thesis: [the small-pain-tolerance variable](../notes/history-repeats-small-pain-tolerance.md) — perfect self-limitation is impossible, so a republic's defense is keeping defection expensive and keeping a culture that re-chooses the constraint.
