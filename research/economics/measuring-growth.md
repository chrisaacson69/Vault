# Measuring Growth — Net Worth vs Transactions
> Economics is about increasing assets over liabilities. But we mostly measure total activity, not whether anyone is better off. GDP counts the transactions, not the improvement.

**Links:** [Value and Profit](./value-and-profit.md), [Equation of Exchange](./equation-of-exchange.md), [Inflation](./inflation.md), [Business Cycles](./business-cycles.md), [Economics](./README.md)

## The Core Problem

Three jokes illustrate the same structural flaw in how we measure economic activity:

### The Three Stooges — Velocity Without Value

Larry owes Moe $20. Moe owes Curly $20. Curly owes Larry $20. They pass a single $10 bill around the circle twice. "Now we're all even!" The $10 bill settled $60 in debt by circling fast enough. High velocity, zero value creation. Nobody's net position changed.

**What it shows:** Velocity (V in MV=PT) can be high while value creation is zero. Counting transactions tells you money moved. It doesn't tell you anyone is better off.

### The Economists and the Manure — GDP Without Improvement

Two economists are walking in the woods. One says, "I'll pay you $100 to eat that pile of manure." He does it. They walk further. The other says, "I'll pay YOU $100 to eat that one." He does it. They realize: they've created $200 in GDP, neither is richer, and they both ate manure.

**What it shows:** GDP measures money changing hands. It doesn't ask whether the activity made anyone's life better. $200 in GDP, zero net improvement, negative lived experience.

### The Change Scam — Transactions Hiding Losses

Customer pays $20 for a $10 item, gets $10 change. Then says "here's another $1, give me a $9 item too." Vendor sees $21 in cash received for ~$19 in merchandise. Looks profitable! But the customer walked away with the $10 change + $19 in goods = $29 value on $21 spent. The vendor lost $8 but the transaction ledger shows $21 in revenue.

**What it shows:** Tracking gross transactions (revenue) instead of net position (profit) lets losses hide in plain sight.

## The Measurement Error

| Metric | Measures | Blind Spot |
|---|---|---|
| **GDP** | Total money changing hands | Whether anyone is better off |
| **Revenue** | Gross inflows | Net position after costs |
| **Transaction volume** | Activity, velocity | Whether value was created or just moved |
| **Money velocity (V)** | How fast money circulates | Whether circulation represents real exchange or just shuffling |
| **Net worth change** | Actual improvement in position | *(This is what matters)* |

The first four are what we mostly measure. The last one is what economics is actually about. The gap between them is where illusions live.

## Real-World Versions

### The Broken Window (Bastiat, 1850)

A kid breaks a shopkeeper's window. The glazier gets paid $200 to fix it. "See? The destruction created economic activity!" GDP agrees — $200 in spending.

But the shopkeeper had a window before and now has a window again. Net change: zero. The $200 he spent on the glazier is $200 he didn't spend on something that would have made him better off (a new suit, expanding his shop, saving for his kid's education). The destruction didn't create wealth — it redirected spending from value-adding to value-restoring. GDP can't see the difference.

**Bastiat's point:** The seen (glazier gets paid) is counted. The unseen (what else the money would have bought) is not. Economics that only measures the seen is systematically biased toward activity and against improvement.

### Healthcare Spending

The US spends ~$4.5 trillion annually on healthcare — ~17% of GDP. GDP loves this. By the transaction metric, healthcare is one of the most productive sectors in the economy.

But Americans aren't healthier than citizens of countries that spend half as much. Much of the spending is administrative overhead, defensive medicine, intermediary costs, and price inflation rather than health improvement. The GDP number measures how much money moves through the healthcare system. It doesn't measure how much health the system produces.

### Litigation

Two companies spend $500K each on lawyers fighting over a $200K contract dispute. That's $1 million in legal GDP. The winner gets $200K. Net result across both parties: -$800K. But GDP recorded a million dollars of "economic activity."

### War

War is the ultimate GDP generator at the ultimate net worth destroyer. Massive government spending, full employment, industrial output at maximum — and cities reduced to rubble. GDP during WWII was enormous. Net wealth of the belligerents was devastated. The postwar recovery then generated more GDP "growth" — rebuilding what was destroyed, which by the net worth measure is just getting back to where you started.

### Monetary Expansion

From [Inflation](./inflation.md) and [Business Cycles](./business-cycles.md): printing money increases transaction volume (more dollars chasing the same goods). GDP in nominal terms goes up. But real wealth — the actual goods and services available — hasn't changed. The Cantillon effect ([Optimal Money Supply](../evolutionary-capitalist/optimal-money-supply.md)) means the new money enriches early recipients at the expense of later ones. Total GDP up, net position of most people down.

## The Austrian Critique

This measurement error is a central Austrian objection to mainstream economics:

- **Mises:** The price system encodes real value information. Aggregate measures (GDP, price indices) destroy the information that makes prices useful — the individual, ordinal, subjective valuations that guide actual economic decisions.
- **Hayek:** The "pretense of knowledge" — macro aggregates give the illusion of understanding while hiding the distributed, local knowledge that actually drives the economy.
- **Bastiat:** Always ask "what is not seen?" The transaction measure captures what happened. It doesn't capture what would have happened instead.
- **Rothbard:** GDP counts government spending as "production" even when it destroys value. A government that taxes $1 trillion and spends it on a war adds $1 trillion to GDP while potentially destroying $10 trillion in real wealth.

## What Should We Measure Instead?

The honest answer: it's hard. Net worth change is the right concept but difficult to aggregate:

- **Household net worth** — total assets minus liabilities across households. Better than GDP but still aggregate.
- **Median real income** — captures whether the typical person is better off. Median avoids the billionaire-distortion problem. Real (inflation-adjusted) avoids the nominal illusion.
- **Consumption of real goods** — are people actually consuming more useful things? (Harder to measure than it sounds — is a $1000 smartphone in 2026 "more" than a $200 phone in 2010?)
- **Bastiat's unseen** — fundamentally unmeasurable. The opportunity cost of misallocated resources can never be directly observed.

The Stooges had the right instinct — check whether you're actually even. They just did the accounting wrong.

## Open Questions

- Is there a principled way to decompose GDP into "value-creating transactions" vs "value-shuffling transactions"? The manure joke is obvious, but where's the line for healthcare spending or financial services?
- Does the [equation of exchange](./equation-of-exchange.md) (MV=PT) inherently hide the net worth question by design? The equation is about flow, not stock.
- How does this connect to the [Weighting Problem](../philosophy/epistemology/weighting-problem.md)? GDP is a composite measure with an implicit weighting function — every dollar of spending counts equally regardless of whether it improved anyone's life.
