A common claim is that “administrative bloat” is the primary driver of rising K–12 costs. The data tells a more nuanced story: non-teaching staff have genuinely proliferated, but most aren’t administrators in the popular sense—they’re counselors, social workers, speech pathologists, and instructional aides, many legally mandated. Meanwhile, administration’s share of spending hasn’t meaningfully grown. The dominant cost drivers are employee benefits, mandated support services, and staffing growth that outpaces enrollment.

1. NCES Expenditure Categories: What Counts as What

The National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) breaks current expenditures (day-to-day operating costs, excluding capital outlay and debt service) into functional categories.

Instruction (~60% of current expenditures)

Salaries and benefits for teachers, textbooks, supplies, and purchased instructional services. This is classroom-level spending.

Support Services (~35–36% of current expenditures)

An umbrella category with seven subfunctions:

Subfunction What It Covers
Student support servicesGuidance counselors, health services, attendance officers, social workers, psychologists, speech pathologists
Instructional staff supportCurriculum development, staff training, libraries, media centers, computer centers
General administrationSchool board (board of education), superintendent’s office, and their immediate central-office staff — district-level governance
School administrationPrincipal’s office — principals, assistant principals, and their building-level office staff
Operations and maintenanceBuilding services (HVAC, custodial), security, grounds, equipment upkeep
Student transportationBus operations, vehicle maintenance
Other support servicesBusiness support (fiscal operations), central support (planning, research, evaluation, data processing, HR, warehousing)

Other Current Expenditures (~4–5%)

Food services, enterprise operations, community services.

Key definitional point: When NCES reports aggregate “administration expenditures,” it typically combines general administration + school administration + other support services (which includes business and central support). This is broader than what most people picture when they hear “administration.” The narrow categories—general administration (superintendent/board) and school administration (principals)—are much smaller.

Sources: NCES CCD Glossary (2018), NCES CCD Glossary (2011), NCES COE: Public School Expenditures.

Critical insight: Spending increases between 2002 and 2020 were not disproportionately absorbed by administration. School admin grew +20.5%, general admin grew +18.3%, and instruction grew +19.9%—all roughly comparable rates. The outlier is pupil support services (+52.7%), driven by mandated services including psychological, guidance, health care, and paraprofessional services for students with disabilities.

The single biggest driver of cost increases was employee benefits: up 81.1% per pupil from 2002 to 2023, versus only 7.7% for salaries. Benefits consumed $3.27 for every $1 added to salaries.

Sources: NCES Fast Facts #66, NCES Press Release (May 2024), Reason Foundation Crossroads Report, Reason Foundation 2025 Spotlight, NCES Digest Table 236.60, NCES Digest Table 236.10, NCES Digest Table 236.75.

The AASA Counterargument

The American Association of School Administrators (AASA) offers a different framing:

The ratio of central-office staff to teachers was 32.4:1 in 1992–93 and 33:1 in 2002–03—essentially unchanged.

Public schools have a staff-to-administrator ratio of roughly 13:1, compared to 3.3:1 in public administration generally, suggesting schools are less top-heavy than government overall.

AASA argues that when all central-office administrative and professional staff are included, there has been “no significant increase” in administrators relative to teachers.

Sources: NCES Digest Table 208.20, NCES Digest Table 208.40, USAFacts, K-12 Dive, American Experiment, AASA, DPE AFL-CIO.

5. What Actually Drives Cost Increases: Claims vs. Data

The “Administrative Bloat” Claim

The popular narrative: schools have hired armies of administrators while neglecting classrooms, and this is the primary driver of rising per-pupil costs.

What the staffing data shows: There has been genuine, dramatic growth in non-teaching staff—up 702% since 1950 vs. 96% for enrollment. District administrative staff specifically grew 74.9% from 2000–2017 vs. 7.7% for teachers. Non-teaching staff now outnumber teachers.

What the spending data shows: Administration spending (narrowly defined) has grown at roughly the same rate as instruction spending—about 18–20% in real per-pupil terms from 2002 to 2020. Administration remains a relatively small share of total spending:

Administration Category % of Current Expenditures Per Pupil (2020)
General administration (superintendent/board)~2%~$265
School administration (principals)~5.6%~$758
Combined~7–8%~$1,023

The FEE/Tabarrok Counterpoint

Alex Tabarrok (George Mason, via FEE) argues the bloat narrative is overstated: principals and administrators number only 0.4 per 100 students—a figure that barely changed from 1950 to 2015. “A bloated little toe cannot explain a 20-pound weight gain.” The real explanation: we hired more teachers (the student-teacher ratio halved from ~27:1 to ~15:1) and pay them more in real terms. The administrator increase was largely complete by 1980, but expenditures kept growing.

The Real Cost Drivers (Ranked by Magnitude)

# Driver Impact
1 Employee benefits (pensions, health insurance) +81.1% per pupil (2002–2023). $3.27 in benefits for every $1 in salary. Pension debt is a primary culprit. NJ highest at $8,333/student. Salaries fell from 59% to 55% of current expenditures (2010–2021); benefits rose from 21% to 24%.
2 Pupil support / special education mandates +52.7% per pupil (2002–2020), from $566 to $864/pupil. Spec. ed. averages $13,127/student; IDEA covers only ~12%. Local gap: $8,160/student. CT: $24,443/pupil; MS: $5,265/pupil.
3 Staffing growth beyond enrollment +15.1% staff for +4.1% students (2002–2023). Enrollment declined ~750K since 2014 while employment grew 600K+. Est. $24.3B/yr excess (Friedman Foundation).
4 Class size reduction Student-teacher ratio halved from ~27:1 (1955) to ~15:1 (2023), roughly doubling the teacher workforce relative to enrollment.
5 Administration (narrowly defined) ~7–8% of spending. Growth rate (~18–20%) roughly matches instruction. District admin staff growth (74.9%, 2000–2017) is real but represents a small absolute base. DeAngelis & Makridis (2025) found growth more pronounced in states with stronger teachers’ union influence.

Bottom line — the nuanced picture:

1. Non-teaching staff have genuinely proliferated—the 702% growth since 1950 is not in dispute.

2. But most of those aren’t “administrators” in the popular sense—they’re counselors, social workers, speech pathologists, instructional aides, health staff, and technology support.

3. Many of these roles are legally mandated—IDEA, Section 504, state mental health requirements, and other mandates drive much of the “support services” growth.

4. The spending data does not show administration taking an ever-larger share—instruction’s share has held steady at 60–61%, and administration’s share hasn’t meaningfully grown.

5. The real cost story is benefits and mandated services—pension/health costs up 81%, pupil support up 53%, while salaries are nearly flat.

6. Teacher pay has actually declined in real terms (−6.1%, 2002–2022) even as total spending per pupil rose 36%.
