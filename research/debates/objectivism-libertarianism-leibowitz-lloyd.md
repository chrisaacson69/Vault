# Objectivism vs. Libertarian Voluntarism — Leibowitz vs. Lloyd
> Two pro-capitalism camps fight over foundations — and the libertarian defends his case by *conceding* subjectivism and grounding it on performative contradiction, the exact move that would have bought him the objectivity he's disclaiming.

**Date:** 2025-02-13
**Source:** [YouTube — The Rational Egoist](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0mIjsB36IRc) — [Transcript](../../raw/debates/objectivism-libertarianism-leibowitz-lloyd-clean.txt)
**Participants:** Michael Leibowitz (Objectivism) vs. Jack V. Lloyd (libertarian voluntarism, author of *Philosophical Voluntarism*)
**Format:** ~77 min, two halves — each defends "my system best defends capitalism," then open exchange. No panel/Q&A.
**Result:** No resolution; collapses (by both debaters' agreement) into a pure epistemology dispute — objective vs. subjective knowledge.
**Vault relevance:** [Relational Objectivity](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md), [The Constitutive/Elective Distinction](../philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md), [The Regress Problem](../philosophy/epistemology/the-regress-problem.md), [Is-Ought and Performative Grounding](../philosophy/morality/is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md), [Stance-Independence Retreat](../philosophy/epistemology/stance-independence-retreat.md), [Wilson vs Objectivism](./wilson-objectivism-stance-dependence.md)

## Context

Leibowitz posted "objectivism offers the best defense of capitalism"; Lloyd objected; this is the debate. The standard frame is Rand's old charge that libertarians lift the politics (NAP, property) without earning it from metaphysics + ethics. That charge is present — but the unusual part is Lloyd's defense, which doesn't reach for natural law at all.

## Argument Structures

### Lloyd — Libertarian Voluntarism (opening + defense)
1. **Define capitalism** as free-market voluntary exchange; private means of production. The question is what *best defends* it.
2. **Self-ownership from performative contradiction.** You can't deny you have a consciousness without using it — "performative contradiction." Establish axioms from how people *act*, the minimums of interaction.
3. **Predicates of existence** → you must acquire things outside the body (food, water, space) to live, so an ethics that forbids this is self-defeating. Grounds the first property right (the body).
4. **Consent ethic.** You cannot *want* your consent violated (performative). So consent is the universal signal of what people do/don't want; bodily and outer property rights are metered through it.
5. **Homesteading** for outer property; scarcity → conflict over scarce physical objects needs metering. Property/consent norms backdrop peaceful trade.
6. **Nothing is self-executing.** No morality strikes you with lightning. Therefore norms are *psychologically adopted*, not objective facts — "social respect," a "bundle of psychological references." Rights = a *just/moral claim* (he accepts the dictionary definition) but cashed out as psychology others must validate.
7. **Subjectivism as humility, not flaw.** We can't escape the Matrix / aren't omniscient, so we can't know *ultimate/metaphysical* truth; we act on best-available perception + cause-effect. Capitalism flourishes when enough people adopt consent/property norms; if they don't, no "ism" or government saves you.

### Leibowitz — Objectivism (critique + opening)
1. **Definitions.** Ethics = a code of (correct, *objective*) values guiding action; a right = a moral claim to something; ownership = a right to a specific thing. Lloyd accepts all three "broad abstract" definitions.
2. **Objective = a truth discoverable by the mind**, a relationship between mind and reality (not omniscience).
3. **Two-part critique of Lloyd:**
   - (a) *Epistemological subjectivism self-destructs* — if there's no objective truth, every claim ("voluntarism is best") is just "my subjective interpretation," with no purchase on the listener. You make a five-minute string of truth claims while denying truth is knowable.
   - (b) *Rights float* — Lloyd starts at self-ownership/property (a moral concept) with **no broader ethical theory** to attach it to. Rights divorced from ethics are "floating in the wind."
4. **Objectivism's stack:** metaphysics (reality exists independent of mind; axioms of existence/identity/consciousness) → epistemology (reason) → ethics (life as the standard; existence-vs-nonexistence alternative; rational self-interest) → rights (right to life → property) → capitalism. Plus a **moral vision** — admiration of achievement, the *fuel* to want a capitalist society.
5. **Rebuttals:** you don't need omniscience to know you hold a can; disagreement among people doesn't bear on the truth of a proposition (flat vs round earth); "Rand just asserts it" is a strawman (Peikoff, books); the Matrix is an arbitrary, evidence-free assertion (Descartes' demon).

### The collapse point
Both explicitly agree near the end that the whole thing reduces to epistemology. Leibowitz cites Hicks's *Explaining Postmodernism*: the postmodernist undercuts his own argument by retreating to "it's all opinion." Lloyd insists he's not contradicting himself — he grants observed facts/cause-effect ("a bridge between both") while denying *ultimate* truth.

## Discussion

**The twist, precisely stated (Chris).** The novelty isn't libertarian-vs-objectivist per se — it's that Lloyd defends libertarianism through a **moral anti-realist** framework *and* rejects the objectivist framework outright.

> "Most Mises-libertarians have no trouble accepting objectivism." — Chris

So Lloyd is an outlier: the usual fusionist libertarian is happy to bolt Randian ethics onto the politics. Lloyd refuses that *and* refuses Rothbard's natural law (he says so explicitly in his opening), grounding instead on performative contradiction + adopted psychology + anti-realism about ultimate truth.

**Chris's verdict on the anti-realism: heavily lacking.** When Leibowitz pressed, Lloyd squirmed each time. The pin-down move Chris flagged as decisive:

> "How can you make statements without accepting those statements are true?"

That's the self-refutation horn, and it landed. Lloyd makes a five-minute string of truth claims (history is violent, kings got away with it, cause-and-effect is real, performance proves existence) while maintaining we can't *know* truth. Leibowitz's "on one hand you say we can't know, on the other you make truth claims, and any assertion I make you dissolve into 'it's all subjective'" is the correct read of the structure — Lloyd is trapped in the subjectivist web he spun.

**The deeper kill shot (Chris): the anti-realism arc is *itself* a performative contradiction.** As a libertarian, Lloyd *knows* performative contradiction — it's the libertarian's own grounding tool (Hoppe's argumentation ethics). He uses it to ground self-ownership and consent. But then his whole *moral anti-realism* is performatively self-defeating on the same logic:

> "Why believe in the NAP?" — Chris

If morality is subjective and non-self-executing, the NAP is just Lloyd's preference — yet he spends the debate arguing people *should* adopt it, that it's the *best* defense of capitalism. Advocacy of a norm performatively presupposes the norm is *correct to adopt*, not merely preferred. You cannot argue "you ought to accept the NAP" while holding "there are no objective oughts." This is a level deeper than Leibowitz's epistemic catch (truth-claims-while-denying-truth): it's a **normative** performative contradiction — denying moral realism *in the act of* prescribing a moral norm. And it's the move Lloyd of all people should have seen, because performative contradiction is exactly how Hoppe's libertarianism grounds the NAP in the first place — as something the arguer cannot coherently deny.

**Closing synthesis (Chris): both camps pre-decide the structural/elective split; the vault lets time decide it.** The two positions reduce to mirror over-commitments:

> - **Objectivist:** "since we can prove *one* moral truth, *everything* is objective."
> - **Libertarian:** "everyone should do what they want — but we all believe in the NAP." — Chris

Each picks the split in advance and then forces every norm to one side. The Objectivist generalizes from a single proven case to total objectivity; the libertarian declares everything elective and then smuggles one mandatory exception (the NAP) it can't justify on its own terms. **The vault's answer is that there are both structural and elective choices — and *time tells us which is which*.** Which norms are constitutive/agent-relative (real, non-arbitrary) versus genuinely elective isn't settled by armchair fiat from either side; it's revealed empirically by [convergence under reality-pressure](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md) over long timescales (Trunk 2 sorting Trunk 1). The error common to Leibowitz and Lloyd is wanting the structural/elective boundary fixed by their epistemology up front, when it's an output of accumulated reality-testing, not an input.

**The root of the divide: the subjective theory of value (Chris).** Why do Objectivists and (Austrian) libertarians clash at all when they agree on so much? Because the libertarian side inherits the **Austrian subjective theory of value** (Mises) — Rand fought Mises over exactly this. Hoppe is the instructive case: he keeps subjective value in *economics* but tries to make property *ethics* objective via argumentation; Lloyd kept the subjectivity and dropped the ethical-objectivity half (which is why he reads as more consistently Misesian than the fusionists who bolt on Objectivism).

**The Objectivists' reverse error — and the grenade test (Chris).** Objectivism over-shoots in the opposite direction: "everything is objective," yet it is a *person's actions* that reveal what he prefers at any given choice. Chris's standing illustration:

> "Does a dad throw himself on a grenade to save his family?" — Chris

There is no third-person *objective* right answer here — and **even Objectivists concede it's only "right" for the person making it** (Rand's own move: dying for those you love can be selfish, because life without them isn't worth living *to you*). That concession *is* agent-relativity — [Category 3](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md). So the Objectivist who says "everything is objective" already lives in the agent-relative middle at the margins, just as the libertarian who says "everything is elective" already smuggles in one mandatory norm. **Both tacitly occupy the constitutive middle they verbally deny — from opposite ends.**

**So they agree more than they disagree (Chris).** Strip the meta-layer and Objectivist, libertarian, and vault all reach the same practical structure — respect property, don't initiate force, the grenade is the dad's call. The disagreement is in *how they get to the answer*, not the answer. Which is the [Agrippa-page lesson](../philosophy/epistemology/the-regress-problem.md) restated: the errors don't live at the foundation, and attacking each other's *route* misses that the *structure* they build is nearly identical.

**Why the argument is immortal (Chris's capstone).** All of this reinforces the vault theory: we *can* see morality emerge out of realism *because of structure* — but the **extent** of that emergence is not fully known, and it **changes when the structure changes**.

> "This is probably why this argument has gone on for most if not all of mankind." — Chris

The boundary between structural (constitutive) and elective is a moving, incompletely-revealed target: still being discovered by convergence over time ([Trunk 2](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md)), and itself shifting when conditions shift — contraception, abundance, AI ([Trunk 4 dynamics](../philosophy/the-four-trunks.md#how-the-trunks-connect)). So the Objectivist-vs-libertarian fight isn't perennial because it's unanswerable; it's perennial because the answer is a *structure-dependent, time-revealed* quantity that no armchair epistemology can fix once and for all. That's the vault's diagnosis of the whole 2,000-year argument in one line.

## Toolkit / Vault Position — the "third man" verdict

The vault is the unseen third party here, and it splits the decision:

- **With Leibowitz on the result, against him on the diagnosis.** Lloyd *does* self-refute — both epistemically (truth-claims while denying knowable truth) and, more deeply, normatively (prescribing the NAP while denying objective oughts). But Leibowitz attributes the failure to denying *mind-independent* truth, and that's the wrong fix. Lloyd's intuition that norms are **mind-dependent** is correct. The repair isn't Rand's mind-independent realism — it's [Category 3 / agent-relative facts](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md): mind-dependent *and* non-arbitrary.
- **Both men share the premise that sinks the debate** — that "objective" must mean "mind-*independent*." Objectivism's two-bucket world (intrinsic-objective vs. mind-dependent-subjective) has no third bucket; Lloyd's anti-realism has none either. So they fight over which bucket norms go in, when the answer is a bucket [neither names](../philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md). A clean specimen of the missing-vocabulary pattern, from *opposite* sides.
- **The portable harvest** (promoted, not trapped here): the *prescribing anti-realist self-refutes* — advocacy performatively presupposes correctness. See [Is-Ought and Performative Grounding § The Prescribing Anti-Realist](../philosophy/morality/is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md#the-prescribing-anti-realist-self-refutes) and the [under-claiming mirror to Hoppe](../philosophy/morality/performative-grounding-lineage.md#the-under-claiming-mirror-lloyd).
- **Lloyd's omniscience bar** is the [Agrippa over-reach](../philosophy/epistemology/the-regress-problem.md): set "objective" at omniscience / escaping the Matrix and *everyone* fails by construction, so call it subjectivism. The vault's reply: you don't have certainty, but what you have still works — agent-relative facts are knowable without omniscience.

## Open Questions

- **Is the "best defense of capitalism" question even well-posed?** Stripped of the epistemology fight, Leibowitz sells *motivational fuel* (admiration of achievement) and Lloyd sells a *low-commitment on-ramp* (adopt consent norms without settling God/ultimate-truth). Different products for different audiences — not obviously commensurable. (Seed not resolved in discussion.)
- **Does Leibowitz ever attack the omniscience *bar* directly,** or only assert around it? He lands "you needn't be omniscient to know you hold a can" but never names why agent-relative facts are objective-yet-mind-dependent — because Objectivism has no Category 3 to point at.
- **Is Lloyd a Misesian outlier or a Misesian purist?** Mises was himself a value-subjectivist; the fusionists who "accept objectivism" paper over that. Worth a separate look at whether Lloyd's performative-grounded subjectivism is the more consistent Misesian position. (Raised in discussion, not pursued.)
