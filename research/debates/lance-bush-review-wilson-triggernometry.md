## The Baseline: Shafer-Landau's Tree

Kaplan's video is a standard academic presentation of Shafer-Landau's metaethics taxonomy from *Whatever Happened to Good and Evil?* It's a decision tree and it's what most students of ethics see first:

```
Are there objective universal moral facts?
├── YES → Moral Objectivism
│   └── Do moral facts depend on consequences?
│       ├── YES → Consequentialism (Bentham)
│       └── NO  → Non-consequentialism (Aristotle, Kant)
└── NO  → Moral Skepticism
    └── Are there non-objective moral facts?
        ├── YES →
        │   └── What determines them?
        │       ├── Individual → Moral Subjectivism
        │       └── Society   → Moral Relativism
        └── NO  → Moral Nihilism
            └── Can moral claims be true or false?
                ├── YES → Error Theory (claims are all false)
                └── NO  → Non-cognitivism (Ayer — not truth-apt)
```

Shafer-Landau adds a realist counter-argument at the end: the **vocabulary-vs-facts distinction.** We invented "planet" but not planets; we invented "right/wrong" but that doesn't entail we invented goodness/evil. The inference from "we made the word" to "we made the thing" is illicit. Shafer-Landau wields this against anti-realism, but the structural move is broader — *mind-dependent vocabulary can still track mind-independent features* — and the vault's constitutive position is closer to this move than it might first appear. More on that below.

The tree's top-level question is the problem. "Objective universal" bundles two independent claims: **grounding** (are moral facts stance-independent?) and **scope** (do they apply to everyone?). A yes/no at the top conflates them. This is the bundle Lance's entire argument is built on.

## Lance's Refinement: Four Orthogonal Axes

Lance's framework makes explicit what the tree forces into ordered branches:

| Axis | Question | Options |
|---|---|---|
| **Grounding** | What makes moral claims true? | Stance-independent / stance-dependent |
| **Semantics** | Do moral claims have truth values at all? | Cognitivism / non-cognitivism |
| **Ontology** | Are there moral facts? | Yes / no |
| **Scope** | To whom does a moral claim apply? | Universalism / relativism |

These axes are orthogonal. You can be stance-dependent-and-universalist (everyone agrees we should maximize utility) or stance-independent-and-non-universalist (moral facts apply differently to different kinds of beings, but independent of any mind). The tree can't cleanly express either — the top-level bundle forces an early collapse.

**Lance's own position:** cognitivist + stance-dependent + moral-facts-exist + scope-variable. Specifically: "stance-dependent cognitivism." Moral claims are propositions with truth values; they're grounded in stances; those stances can be individual or collective; both produce genuine moral facts, just not mind-independent ones.

**Wilson's position** (per Lance's reading): cognitivist + stance-independent + moral-facts-exist + universalist. The standard realist cell.

Wilson's metaethics error, on Lance's account, is inferring from "stance-dependent" to "no moral facts" — a collapse of **grounding** into **ontology**. Someone who rejects stance-independent moral facts still has moral facts if they're a stance-dependent cognitivist; Wilson treats the rejection of the first as rejection of the second.

## What Lance Gets Right

Three points the vault should concede cleanly rather than collapse them back in defense of realism.

**1. Grounding and ontology are orthogonal, and Wilson conflates them.** Stance-dependent cognitivism is not error theory is not non-cognitivism. Wilson's repeated move — "if morality is stance-dependent, you can't do anything immoral" — requires the conflation to work. Once the axes are separated, the move fails: stance-dependent standards can be violated, contradicted, and argued against just like any other normative standards.

**2. Stance-dependent views allow moral error.** Lance gives three routes:

- *Wrong about what you value.* The Green Eggs and Ham example — the character insists he doesn't like green eggs and ham, then discovers he does. People can be wrong about their own values. Values can be as much a matter of discovery as any other fact.
- *Stance-dependent standards depend on non-moral facts.* A utilitarian who values maximizing happiness can be wrong about which actions actually maximize happiness. The moral framework is stance-dependent; the empirical facts it refers to aren't.
- *Acting inconsistently with your own standards.* A person who holds that causing unnecessary suffering is wrong and then acts in ways that do so is wrong by their own standards.

All three are correct and the vault shouldn't deny them. The common "anti-realists can't be wrong about morality" objection is indeed lazy.

**3. Scope ≠ grounding.** "Universal" and "stance-independent" are different claims. Wilson uses them interchangeably; they aren't. You can have stance-independent moral facts that apply non-universally (different kinds of beings have different moral obligations, but those obligations are mind-independent), and you can have stance-dependent facts that apply universally (every culture independently converges on "don't murder"). Keeping these distinct is a real contribution and the vault's own [stance-independence retreat](../philosophy/epistemology/stance-independence-retreat.md) page should absorb it.

## Where Lance Slides Into Word-Games

Three places the technical literacy does rhetorical rather than substantive work.

**1. "Stance-dependent facts are still facts."** Technically correct. But it does rhetorical work: it preserves the word "fact" while the substance has shifted. Someone who's been arguing against "moral facts" in Wilson's sense finds out Lance means something different by "facts," and now they appear to be agreeing with him. The whole *point* of Wilson's dispute is about what "moral facts" means in the first place — what makes a claim count as a fact rather than a preference. Lance's "still facts" move side-steps that dispute by stipulating a broader definition of "fact" and then scoring Wilson for not using Lance's definition.

This isn't dishonest — it's a legitimate terminological move. But it doesn't settle the underlying disagreement; it renames it.

**2. "Morality is like price negotiation."** Lance's positive analogy: there's no objectively correct car price, there's what buyer and seller agree on. Scale up to civilizations and that's moral discourse. This is illuminating for many moral questions — conventions about manners, coordination problems, contract law, most positive morality.

It breaks for constitutive cases. Price negotiation already presupposes property, non-violence, consent, and recognition of the counterparty as an agent — features you can't negotiate without. Using negotiation as the *meta-model* of morality uses something morally loaded to explain morality. The features that make negotiation possible in the first place aren't themselves up for negotiation. Lance's analogy is question-begging for exactly the cases where the vault's constitutive move applies.

**3. "Most atheist philosophers are moral realists."** Lance cites the 2020 PhilPapers survey: ~50% of academic philosophers are atheist-realists; only ~26% are anti-realists. The move is an appeal to authority against the Wilson-adjacent claim that atheism implies anti-realism. That specific claim does deserve pushback — but the survey data doesn't advance the underlying argument. It redistributes the prior without engaging the reasoning on either side.

## The Self-Critique Gap

Two places Lance's framework doesn't notice its own problems.

**1. "Why should I care about stance-independent moral facts?"** Lance's challenge: even granting that stance-independent moral facts exist, what's the motivational force? He says "I can just not care." If he means this as a Humean is-ought point — facts don't entail motivations — it's deep and serious. If he means it as a knock-down argument against realism, it's self-undermining.

The "should" in "why should I care" is doing the work. If there's no real "should" — if we're fully in the anti-realist frame — there's no determinate answer to the question either. The rhetorical force depends on the normative framing he's explicitly rejecting. An anti-realist who truly thought the question "why should I care about anything" had no stance-independent answer would also have to accept that the question "why should I care about stance-independent moral facts" has no stance-independent answer — including no stance-independent answer that cuts against caring.

This isn't a fatal blow to anti-realism; the Humean point about motivation is real. But Lance deploys the move as if it settles something, and it only settles something when it's smuggling in the normative frame he's denying.

**2. Condemning ≠ causing.** Lance argues, correctly, that anti-realism doesn't *cause* bad behavior — a person can be a bad person and rationalize it with anti-realism as easily as the reverse. The move is defensive: don't blame the framework for the user.

But that's orthogonal to whether anti-realism can *coherently condemn* bad behavior. Lance slides from the first to the second without examining the transition. He condemns Wilson throughout the video — Wilson is wrong, Wilson's objections are lazy, Wilson should apologize — and the condemnation isn't "Wilson violates my stance-dependent standards and therefore is wrong *by my lights*." It's presented as if there's a real fact of the matter. A stance-dependent framework can say "Wilson's claims are false relative to my standards" but the moral *force* of Lance's condemnation reads as stance-independent in practice. He's doing what he accuses Wilson of bundling.

This is the weakest corner of Lance's framework and he doesn't seem to see it.

## The Vault's Constitutive Axis — What Neither Tree Reaches

Kaplan's tree organizes the space around one question: "what makes moral claims true?" Lance's four axes do the same — grounding, semantics, ontology, scope are all variations of "what is the truth-maker?" Both taxonomies live inside the truth-maker frame.

The vault's [constitutive-elective distinction](../philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md) is organized around a different question: **"what moral features are conditions of the possibility of acting at all?"** This is orthogonal to everything in Kaplan's tree and Lance's grid.

- **Mind-dependent but non-negotiable.** The constitutive features are mind-dependent in the sense that they require agents (you can't have moral obligations without someone to have them). In that sense the vault is not stance-independent in Kaplan's or Wilson's sense. But the features are also not stance-dependent in Lance's sense, because you can't coherently negotiate out of them — negating them requires acting, which requires them. Lance's "I can just not care" move can't be coherently performed at this layer; the "not caring" would itself presuppose the agency conditions it's denying.
- **Shafer-Landau's vocabulary/facts move is a partial ally.** His point — *we made the word "planet" but not planets* — is the same structural move the vault makes with *we required agents for morality, but the structural requirements of agency aren't arbitrary*. Mind-dependent referent does not entail mind-arbitrary referent. Shafer-Landau grounds the non-arbitrariness in stance-independent facts; the vault grounds it in agency structure. Different groundings, but the shape of the move — "you can't collapse 'we made the concept' into 'the referent is contingent'" — is the same.
- **Performative grounding does the heavy lifting.** [Performative grounding](../philosophy/morality/performative-grounding-lineage.md) is the 2,400-year tradition (Aristotle through Korsgaard) that treats moral constraints as the conditions of rational agency, not as independent facts about the universe. Neither Kaplan nor Lance engages with this tradition in the reviewed material. It's the missing axis in both taxonomies.

So the honest read of Lance's review: he catches Wilson on real conflations within the standard truth-maker frame, wins those specific rounds, and never sees that the vault's move happens outside that frame entirely. The "just not care" challenge assumes that any moral feature must either be an external fact you can decline to honor or a stance you construct. A constitutive feature is neither. The framework literally doesn't have the category.

## Graphs vs. Trees, at Two Scales

Shafer-Landau's tree fails because the underlying relations are multi-axis and a tree forces one axis to dominate. Lance's grid improves by making the axes explicit but still organizes them around a single question (the truth-maker). The constitutive axis adds a dimension neither taxonomy has room for.

This is structurally the same problem the vault's own design solves. A folder hierarchy imposes containment on knowledge where the underlying relations cross domains — morality connects to economics, game theory, psychology, politics, none hierarchically. The vault's answer is cross-links as first-class structure: any page can link to any other, and the graph preserves the multi-axis relations the folder tree would collapse.

The same pattern repeats across the vault's work. [Planner-LM composites](../planner-lm-composites.md) avoid the tree-shaped "is it the LLM or not" question by decomposing into state model + value model + planner + dialogue layer — four roles, graph-connected. [Monopoly's trade engine](../gaming/monopoly/subgraph-trade-engine-spec.md) uses subgraph analysis precisely because trade relations aren't hierarchical. The [constitutive-elective distinction](../philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md) adds a dimension to a debate stuck in the objective/subjective tree.

**The general pattern: hierarchies privilege one axis; graphs preserve multiple axes.** When a field's arguments repeatedly stall or conflate, the stall is often a tree being asked to represent a graph. The unblocking move is usually to identify the hidden axes and restructure as a cross-product. That's what Lance does to Wilson, that's what the vault does to Lance, and that's what the vault's own form does to the folder hierarchy underneath it.

## Open Questions

- Is the constitutive-elective axis visible to trained meta-ethicists who aren't already in the performative-grounding tradition? Lance is trained and doesn't see it; David Enoch (upcoming debate) might or might not. If it's invisible to specialists, is the vault's position a genuine contribution or a private language?
- Does Lance's price-negotiation analogy actually fail for constitutive cases, or does it just fail if you *start* from a constitutive frame? The argument as stated depends on negotiation presupposing morality; someone who thinks all moral features are negotiable in principle would reject the premise.
- Would Lance accept the Shafer-Landau vocabulary/facts move in a weaker form — "mind-dependent concepts can track non-arbitrary features" — and if so, does that open the door to the constitutive position even without granting agency-structure grounding?
