# The SAVE Act and the Suffrage Question
> Voter ID is the easy part. The hard part is whether universal suffrage is structurally compatible with limited government.

**Date:** ~2025
**Source:** [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aBWT1lgHSv4) — [Transcript](../../raw/debates/save-act-transcript.txt)
**Format:** Solo video essay (~33 minutes)
**Speaker:** Self-identified Rothbardian/Hoppian libertarian (Oregon-based)
**Vault relevance:** [scope confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md), [civilizational cycles](../philosophy/civilizational-cycles/README.md), [the Gödel governance problem](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md), [opposing forces](../philosophy/opposing-forces.md), [economics](../economics/README.md)

## Context

The video is structured in two parts. The first (~24 minutes) is a straightforward defense of the SAVE Act — voter ID legislation requiring proof of citizenship to register and ID to vote. This portion is competent and largely correct. The second part (~9 minutes) is where the real ideas are: a Hoppian argument that universal suffrage itself is the structural problem, and that the SAVE Act doesn't go far enough.

The original interest is in the suffrage argument, not the SAVE Act specifics. The speaker's Rothbard/Hoppe identification is explicit by the 23-minute mark ("those of us on the far libertarian right like Hoppians like myself") and detectable earlier through the property-rights-first lens and "empiricist fallacy" framing.

---

## Part 1: SAVE Act Defense (Summary)

The speaker refutes common objections to the SAVE Act. This portion is solid and doesn't need deep analysis:

**"Elections are already safe"** — The fox analogy: if you only use a camera, you find fewer foxes than if you also look for tracks. Less detection ≠ less fraud. Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence. Mail-in voting without ID is structurally unauditable. Correct.

**"Voter ID is racist" (equity fallacy)** — Disparate impact ≠ discrimination. The same logic that makes voter ID "racist" (poor people have harder time getting ID, minorities are disproportionately poor) also makes alcohol ID requirements "racist" — and the critic explicitly says those aren't. Inconsistency exposed. Also correct: the SAVE Act doesn't specify which IDs, it leaves implementation to states, and a passport card costs ~$65 for 10 years (~50 cents/month).

**"Married women can't vote"** — Propaganda. The Act requires states to have processes for document discrepancies (name changes). Marriage certificates satisfy this. The Center for American Progress's claim that the Act "doesn't mention" this is a lie of omission — the Act leaves implementation to states rather than naming specific documents. Correct.

**"Jim Crow 2.0"** — Rhetoric, not argument. No discriminatory targeting in the bill text. Even some Democrats (Fetterman) break rank on this framing. Correct.

**Libertarian 4th Amendment objection** — The speaker's most interesting pre-suffrage point: even in a Hoppian private city, you'd have ID (private ID to distinguish payers from non-payers). Voting isn't a libertarian right. The SAVE Act is government regulating *itself*, not regulating citizens. And it *reduces* government power by reducing the number of votes that can authorize state expansion. This is a clean Hoppian reframe.

## Part 2: The Suffrage Argument (The Meat)

### The Argument Chain

1. **Democracy is an economic system.** Instead of voluntary exchange where people spend resources on what they choose, democracy is majority-rule resource allocation regardless of what the minority wants. Clean insight.

2. **Unqualified voters exist.** The 7-year-old test: everyone agrees children shouldn't vote because they'd vote for "free french fries, lemonade, and videos" (quoting Hoppe). Once you admit *some* qualification is legitimate, you've broken the "universal suffrage is sacred" frame.

3. **The qualification criterion should be net taxpayer status.** Those who don't pay into the system have no incentive to understand it and disproportionately vote for "free stuff" — expanding the state at others' expense. This is the free-rider problem applied to democratic participation.

4. **The founders understood this.** Property-based suffrage and direct tax requirements were the original filters. Government spending was 4-7% of GDP in the 1800s. Universal suffrage removed the filter. Government spending is now ~40% of GDP.

5. **Therefore:** Voting should be a privilege tied to contribution, not a universal right. The SAVE Act's real problem is that it doesn't go far enough.

### The Rothbard Contradiction

The speaker cites Rothbard's democracy paradox: can a majority vote to end democracy? If yes, democracy is self-defeating. If no, then not all decisions are actually democratic — which means the system already admits that majority rule has limits. This is the [Gödel Governance Problem](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md) in one sentence.

## Assessment

### Where he's right

1. **Democracy as economic system** — framing voting as resource allocation rather than sacred civic ritual is correct and clarifying. It strips the mysticism and exposes the mechanism.

2. **The 7-year-old test** — genuinely effective. Forces any opponent to admit that *some* voter qualification is legitimate, which means the debate is about *where the line is*, not *whether there should be one*.

3. **The free-rider problem is real** — voters who bear no cost of the policies they vote for have perverse incentives. This is basic economics applied to governance, and it's correct.

4. **The GDP ratchet** — 4-7% → 40% is a real number tracking a real problem. Whatever the cause, something structural changed, and universal suffrage is at minimum correlated.

5. **The Rothbard contradiction** — democracy's self-referential vulnerability is a genuine structural flaw, not a rhetorical trick.

6. **"Fairness vs. works"** — the vault's measurement-over-sentiment principle applied to governance. Whether something is "fair" is separate from whether it produces good outcomes.

### Where he's incomplete

1. **"Stakeholder" is broader than "net taxpayer."** The renter has a stake in zoning. The minimum-wage worker has a stake in labor law. The non-property-owning parent has a stake in school policy. Defining stake as fiscal contribution excludes people who are *materially affected by* decisions without *funding* them. A person constrained by the system is a stakeholder in the system, whether they're a net payer or not.

2. **Restricting the vote doesn't fix the structural flaw.** The founders had property-based suffrage and got a government that served property owners. Universal suffrage expanded the capture pool. Net-taxpayer suffrage would re-narrow it. But in all three cases, the incentive to *use the vote to take from others* remains — only the beneficiary changes. The mechanism is the same. Restricting who votes changes who captures the system, not whether it gets captured.

3. **The 4-7% → 40% GDP correlation isn't cleanly attributable to suffrage.** Two world wars, the New Deal, the Cold War, Medicare/Medicaid, the military-industrial complex — all contributed. The expansion of the franchise is *one* factor, not *the* factor. The speaker treats the correlation as near-causal without controlling for anything.

4. **The historical comparison is selective.** The 1800s with restricted suffrage also had slavery, no labor protections, child labor, and company towns. "Government spending was lower" is true, but the full picture of that era isn't straightforwardly better — it's better on *one metric* (fiscal restraint) and worse on others (individual liberty for non-property-owners).

5. **The solution assumes the problem is who votes rather than what they can vote on.** This is the key vault divergence. See below.

## Vault Response: Restrict the Scope, Not the Vote

The speaker's diagnosis is correct: democracy has a structural free-rider problem that incentivizes voting for state expansion. But his prescription (restrict who votes) treats the symptom rather than the cause.

The vault's position: **the problem isn't who votes — it's what the vote can reach.**

If the government can't do X regardless of the vote count, then unqualified voters voting for X doesn't matter. Constitutional limits, principled gates, separation of powers — these restrict the *scope* of democratic authority rather than the *participation* in it. The founders' real insight wasn't property-based suffrage. It was the *friction structure* that made it hard to turn votes into confiscation.

Universal suffrage didn't break the system — the erosion of constitutional limits did. The ratchet isn't "too many voters." It's "too few limits on what voters can authorize."

This maps directly to [scope confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md):

| Approach | What it restricts | Structural effect |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Net-taxpayer suffrage** | Who participates | Changes who captures the system, not whether it gets captured |
| **Constitutional friction** | What the vote can touch | Limits the capture surface regardless of who votes |
| **Federalism/polycentrism** | At what scale decisions are made | Shrinks the free-rider problem by shrinking the governance unit |

The honest assessment: **none of these are sufficient.** The constitution with federalism with separation of powers with a near-impossible amendment process still failed to prevent regulatory capture. Friction structures slow the ratchet but don't stop it. This is the [Gödel governance problem](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md): any system powerful enough to govern is powerful enough to be captured.

The question isn't "how do we stop the ratchet?" It's "how do we maximize the time between resets?" — which is the [civilizational cycles](../philosophy/civilizational-cycles/README.md) question.

**Small government isn't a number — it's a scope constraint.** The government touches only what passes the principled gate, and the gate is hard to move. The 5% → 40% GDP ratchet is evidence that the gate moved too easily, not that the wrong people were voting.

## Alternative Mechanisms (Percolating)

Several structural alternatives preserve broad participation while attacking the actual flaw:

1. **Friction, not exclusion** — the vault's existing answer. Restrict what the vote can reach, not who can cast it. Constitutional limits as the principled gate.

2. **Futarchy / prediction markets** (Robin Hanson) — vote on *values* (what outcomes we want), bet on *policies* (which methods achieve those outcomes). Separates the "what do we want" question (universal participation legitimate) from the "how do we get it" question (expertise and skin-in-the-game matter). Interesting but demands high citizen engagement.

3. **Subsidiarity / polycentric governance** (Ostrom) — push decisions to the lowest viable scale. Local decisions made locally, where everyone *is* a stakeholder. The free-rider problem shrinks as the governance unit shrinks. This is federalism — the vault already agrees here.

4. **Quadratic voting** (Glen Weyl) — budget of vote-credits; casting multiple votes on one issue costs quadratically more. Lets people express intensity of preference without factional dominance. Market mechanism applied to participation.

**Shared constraint:** All of these (except #1) require more citizen engagement with governance, which creates a tension — government was formed to *distribute labor*, not to be a full-time civic obligation. Any system that demands deep policy engagement from every citizen is asking them to do the thing government was supposed to handle. The more democratic accountability you build in, the more you demand from people who just want to live their lives.

This remains an open problem.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the ratchet stoppable or only delayable?** If the Gödel governance problem is structural, then all governance systems decay — the only variable is rate. Does the vault accept this, and if so, what follows?
2. **Can constitutional friction be made self-reinforcing?** The amendment process was supposed to be nearly impossible. It was bypassed through judicial interpretation and regulatory expansion instead. Is there a friction structure that resists *all* modes of erosion, not just the formal one?
3. **Is the 5% → 40% GDP ratchet primarily a suffrage problem, a constitutional erosion problem, or an economic complexity problem?** The speaker attributes it to suffrage. The vault leans toward constitutional erosion. But increasing economic complexity (requiring more coordination infrastructure) may be a third independent factor.
4. **What does "small government" actually mean?** The vault uses the term without defining it. It probably means: *government whose scope is constrained to what passes the principled gate*. But the principled gate itself needs formal criteria — and those criteria are the [scope legitimacy criteria](../../tasks/active.md) still on the todo list.
