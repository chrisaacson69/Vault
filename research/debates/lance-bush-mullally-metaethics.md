# Lance Bush vs. Ryan Mullally — Moral Realism vs. Global Anti-Realism
> A cordial 3.4-hour metaethics conversation in which a hardline anti-realist concedes a deist's theistic metaethic is genuinely realist — and the real action is that *both men wield the same constitutive/unintelligibility move toward opposite conclusions.*

**Date:** 2026-05-25
**Source:** [YouTube — Natural Law with Ryan Mullally](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J6ly6S980Q8) — [Transcript](../../raw/debates/yt-J6ly6S980Q8.transcript.txt)
**Participants:** Lance Bush (global normative anti-realist; experimental-philosophy PhD, metaethics) vs. Ryan Mullally (deist / natural-law moral realist; lawyer, "Natural Law" channel)
**Format:** Long-form cordial conversation (not a scored debate); definitions → Lance's view → Ryan's positive case → audience Q&A
**Duration:** ~3h22m
**Result:** No decisive clash. Lance concedes Ryan's theistic metaethic is *structurally* realist on its own terms (while rejecting the theism). Both agree secular/naturalist realism and non-naturalist "external reasons" accounts are weak. Ryan's retrospective: Lance's position is a self-undermining "non-position."

## Context

This is the *anti-realist-vs-realist* pairing the vault's metaethics cluster had been missing. Prior pages mostly orbit Andrew Wilson's presuppositional realism; here a professional anti-realist (Bush — likely the single biggest popularizer of "stance-independent" in YouTube philosophy) meets a *natural-law* realist whose realism is explicitly theistic. The conversation is exploratory and gracious, which is its main structural limitation: the sharpest objections on both sides are flagged and then not pressed.

## Argument structures

### Lance Bush — global normative anti-realism
1. **Realism** = there is ≥1 *stance-independent* moral fact. A **stance** = any evaluative standpoint, real *or hypothetical*, not necessarily psychological (the Bible, or ChatGPT-generated rules, count as stances).
2. He is anti-realist about **every** normative domain — moral, epistemic, **logical**, prudential, aesthetic. No stance-independent reason to be logical or rational; violating logic is a *practical/social* faux pas (playing chess by the wrong rules), not a factual error.
3. **Preferences are constitutive of how he analyzes "ought."** So "ought you prefer your stronger preference?" is not *false* but *unintelligible / a loaded question* — it tries to apply an "ought" from outside the only frame ("ought" is cashed out in) that gives the word content.
4. **Universal agreement ≠ stance-independence** (the French-fries analogy): even all humans who ever lived agreeing would be, at most, indirect evidence.
5. **Practical-equivalence thesis:** realists and anti-realists act identically, because everyone only ever acts on preferences; a realist complies with stance-independent facts only insofar as he *subjectively values* doing so. So the distinction has little practical import.
6. **The motivation problem** (his strongest blade): for a *secular* realist, why follow stance-independent facts that don't align with your values? Theistic realism escapes via *practical* reasons (God knows best; God can enforce).
7. **Unintelligibility thesis:** non-naturalist "external reasons" (Parfit/Scanlon/Watkins) are *not even false* — "reason" used as an unanalyzable primitive ("a reason is what counts in favor… nothing more to say") is a category/grammatical error, like "an animal *simpliciter*" or "intrinsically taller."

### Ryan Mullally — deist / natural-law realism
1. **Bare theism:** God is omnibenevolent and unchanging ⇒ "God is good" is at least one *stance-independent evaluative* moral fact (if God's goodness is independent of all stances, including God's own). Index morality to the perfect good; other indices are *errors*.
2. **Natural law fits the pivotal moments:** Nuremberg, abolition, civil rights, suffrage all invoked transcendent law. Realist-sounding language lines up with those moments → a *comparative advantage* for realism (rhetorical, if not philosophical).
3. **Concedes the is–ought gap on naturalism:** you can't get a transcendent ought from naturalist is; Sam-Harris-style naturalist realism is weak/near-incompatible. His realism is therefore *parasitic on his theism* — and he brackets God's existence for this metaethics-only frame.

## Analysis — key exchanges

### The "greater good" gambit fails to a constitutive move
Ryan offers "one ought prefer the greater good" (leaving *good* to be filled in) as a candidate stance-independent norm. Lance dissolves it: if you let the agent fill the blank, they fill it stance-dependently; pressed to "ought you prefer your stronger preference?" the question is either *trivial* (= "do you prefer what you prefer more?") or *unintelligible*. **This is a constitutive dissolution, not a refutation** — and it's the structural heart of the whole conversation (see below).

### "What's the utility of the definition?" — a good pragmatist jab
Ryan presses: if all-humans-ever-agreeing is *still* stance-dependent, what work does the concept do? Lance: conceptual ≠ empirical question; useful here-and-now where people disagree (like "atheism" in a hypothetically all-theist world). Decent — but it leaves a real tension: a hardline conceptual distinction with *conceded minimal practical import* sits awkwardly beside Lance's own pragmatism + quietism.

### Normative entanglement / pragmatic cancellation
Lance's empirical contribution: "Do you think it's objectively wrong to X?" fuses a *normative* and a *metaethical* question. "No" is ambiguous across three positions, one of which sounds monstrous. The needle-threading answer *cancels* the bad implication ("No — because nothing is objectively wrong — but it *is* morally wrong, repugnant, and nobody should do it"). His pilot study: that answer reads almost as well as plain "yes." A genuinely useful diagnostic of why realists gain unearned rhetorical advantage.

### Theism passes Lance's own realism test — instantly
The surprise (Ryan's words): Lance applies *none* of his famous trilemma (realist positions are **false, incoherent, or trivial**) to the theistic metaethic, conceding it's realist *on its own terms* (false-for-Lance only because he rejects the theism). The quiet result: **anti-realism's best case is specifically against *secular* realism; theistic realism is the one realism that structurally survives** — because it answers the motivation problem the secular realist can't.

## The deep structural observation (the gold)

**Both men deploy the same weapon — the constitutive / unintelligibility move — toward opposite conclusions.**
- Lance: "preferences are *constitutive* of how I analyze 'ought,' therefore the realist's question is unintelligible." He dissolves the realist's question by showing it presupposes a frame it then tries to step outside.
- The vault's constitutive-realism trunk (the [performative-grounding lineage](../philosophy/morality/performative-grounding-lineage.md); Wilson's presuppositional move) runs the *identical* structure to *ground* realism: the skeptic's question presupposes the very norms it tries to question.

Same move, opposite targets. The [Lance Bush ↔ Wilson page](./lance-bush-review-wilson-triggernometry.md) already flagged "the constitutive axis neither taxonomy reaches" — **here Lance operates explicitly on that axis**, which is why a constitutive realist and a constitutive anti-realist mostly talk *past* each other: each can run a parity-of-reasoning dissolution of the other ([internal-critique-vs-self-defeat](./wilson-triggernometry-christian-nationalism.md) is the same shape). The constitutive move doesn't settle realism; it relocates the disagreement to *which* frame is the inescapable one.

## Where the vault stands

The vault's metaethics ([constitutive/elective](../philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md), [relational objectivity](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md), [stance-independence retreat](../philosophy/epistemology/stance-independence-retreat.md)) **refuses the single axis this debate is fought on.** It splits "stance-(in)dependent" into two questions: *does it require minds?* (both men: yes) and *is it constitutive or elective?* (the fight nobody names). Morality lives in the cell the old vocabulary has no word for — **mind-dependent AND constitutive**.

Reposition the debaters on that grid:
- **Lance is a mind-dependent *electivist*** — morality bottoms out in preferences, agents "function fine without it," convergence is "just biology + game theory." That is the vault's *elective case*, nearly verbatim. He calls it "anti-realism" only because the academy hands him a one-axis menu.
- **The vault is a mind-dependent *constitutivist*** — morality is entailed by the structure of agency; the is–ought gap is crossed by [performative grounding](../philosophy/morality/is-ought-and-performative-grounding.md); convergence-under-reality-pressure is the diagnostic.

So **"realism vs anti-realism" is, in vault terms, a mislabeled proxy for "constitutive vs elective."** Where the vault beats Lance's strongest moves:
- **The fries move conflates categories 3 and 4.** Universal love of fries ≠ objectivity — true, but fries are *elective* (category 4). Morality is *cyanide* (category 3): stance-dependent in Lance's sense, yet convergent, error-admitting, and causally consequential. Toxicology isn't "preferences all the way down."
- **The unintelligibility thesis lands — and the vault dodges it.** Parfit's brute "external reasons" are empty; the vault agrees, because *its* reasons are statable — entailed by agency, not posited as primitives.
- **The motivation problem ("why comply?") is the empty seat both men declared unfillable.** They agreed only theism answers it. The vault answers it *secularly* via constitutive grounding: you can't opt out because the norm is a precondition of your own acting; non-compliance is self-undermining, not preference-mismatch. **The vault is the secular realism the debate said couldn't exist** — threaded between Ryan's theism and Parfit's unintelligible reasons.

**The corollary:** vault and Lance agree on almost everything (the binary is broken; morality is mind-dependent; category-4 is genuinely subjective; brute reasons are empty) and disagree on *one* near-empirical question — **does biology + game theory fully explain moral convergence, or is there a residue only reality-tracking explains?** The metaphysical chasm shrinks to that.

**And the debate is evidence the split is well-founded — by the split's own criterion.** The framework *predicts the shape of the conversation*: if the standard axis is a broken proxy, debaters on it should talk past each other, keep finding "strange-bedfellows" agreement, land no decisive blow, and never locate their real disagreement — which is exactly what happens. Stronger: a hostile professional metaethicist (Bush) and the vault, working independently and reaching opposite first-order conclusions, **both reject the realism/anti-realism framing as misleading.** By the vault's own [Trunk-2](../philosophy/the-four-trunks.md#trunk-2--verification-epistemology) standard — independent convergence with no shared cause but the structure — that agreement *that the axis misleads* is evidence the axis really is broken. *(Honest caveat: the convergence is on the negative claim only — Bush diagnoses the bad labels but stays on one axis and abandons realism; the vault adds the second axis and keeps a constitutive realism. Shared diagnosis, divergent cure.)* It's the verification-layer thesis in the metaethics domain: an accumulated framework, applied to fresh input it didn't generate, locates the confusion the unaided reader couldn't.

## Structural problems

**Lance.** (1) The "preferences are constitutive → your question is unintelligible" move risks unfalsifiability: a realist can run the mirror move ("your 'it all bottoms out in preferences' is itself a stance-dependent *stipulation*, not a discovered fact"). Ryan never presses this. (2) Genuine tension between *hardline* conceptual anti-realism and *quietist+pragmatist* "no substantive difference": if realism/anti-realism make no practical difference, why be a militant anti-realist rather than a *realism quietist* (he is already a free-will quietist)? Ryan names this as the closing charge — the position looks like a "non-position, preferences all the way down," which invites the self-reference worry: on what stance-independent ground is *anti-realism* worth arguing for?

**Ryan.** His realism is entirely downstream of an *undischarged* premise (God, bracketed for the frame). Valid as "given my metaphysic, realism follows" — but it's a defense of *theistic* realism, not moral realism per se, and he concedes he can't defend the secular/naturalist version at all. The natural-law "fits the pivotal moments" point is a *rhetorical*-advantage argument that he himself flags is not a truth-maker.

## Toolkit (for engaging these arguments later)

- **Separate the two questions.** When someone asks "is it *objectively* wrong?", split the normative claim ("it's wrong") from the metaethical one ("there are stance-independent facts"). Don't let a "no" on the second be heard as a "no" on the first (pragmatic cancellation).
- **Watch for the constitutive dissolution.** "That question is unintelligible / loaded" is a powerful move — but note it *relocates* rather than *settles*, and the other side can usually run it in reverse. Ask: *which frame is being claimed as inescapable, and why that one?*
- **Press the secular/theistic split.** Anti-realism's motivation problem bites *secular* realism hardest; theistic realism answers it. Don't defend "moral realism" in the abstract — defend the specific version whose grounding survives the motivation question.
- **"Can you say what it means?"** Bush's unintelligibility test (a central theoretical concept whose holder can only offer synonyms is a red flag) is a reusable scalpel — but apply it to *both* sides: "preference," "stance," and "constitutive" deserve the same demand for content as "external reason."

## Open questions

- Is the constitutive move *symmetric*? If both realist and anti-realist can dissolve the other's question by declaring their own frame inescapable, what (if anything) breaks the tie — fruitfulness? parsimony? lived inescapability? This is the unresolved core.
- Does Bush's *global* anti-realism (incl. logic/epistemics) self-undermine, or is "I have no stance-independent obligation to be logical, I just prefer to be" a stable position? Ryan asserts the former; Bush treats it as obviously the latter. Neither argues it through.
- Is "theistic realism survives, secular realism doesn't" a *general* result, or an artifact of Bush's particular (motivation-centered) objections?
