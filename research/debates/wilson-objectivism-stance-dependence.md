# Andrew Wilson vs Objectivism: Stance Dependence and the Grounding of Rights
> Can moral facts be "stance dependent" and still be facts? The Objectivist says yes; Wilson says no. Both miss the structural issue.

**Date:** ~2025 (undated panel debate)
**Source:** [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DhpfsOSNakY) — [Transcript](../../raw/debates/debate-transcript-wilson-objectivism.txt)

## Context

Andrew Wilson — a theistic moral realist who has appeared across multiple vault debates — faces Objectivists on Eduardo's TikTok panel. The original prompts were broad bait (healthcare as a right, socialism good, capitalism bad), but the real exchange that emerged is about whether Objectivist ethics can ground moral facts at all. The primary Objectivist voice is ProphetDivinityy; Eduardo owns the channel but doesn't speak in this clip.

Wilson's strategy: push on "stance dependence" until the Objectivists either admit their facts aren't stance-independent (undermining "objectivity") or claim stance-independence (which Wilson thinks requires God).

ProphetDivinityy argues from Ayn Rand's framework: moral facts are grounded in the requirements of human nature to flourish. Rights are moral principles sanctioning freedom of action in a social context. Force should only be used to stop force. These are facts about *what humans need to live*, and they are facts whether or not anyone recognizes them — but they are "stance dependent" in the precise sense that they are *relational* (they involve agents and their relationship to reality).

This creates the central impasse: Wilson treats "stance dependent" as "subjective opinion." ProphetDivinityy treats it as "relational fact." Neither clearly distinguishes the two.

## Key Arguments — Analysis

### 1. The Stance-Dependence Impasse (The Entire Debate)

**Wilson's move:** Get the Objectivist to admit moral facts are "stance dependent." Then declare that stance-dependent things aren't really facts — they're just opinions.

**ProphetDivinityy's defense:** Stance dependence ≠ arbitrariness. "I'm taller than you" is stance dependent (it's a relationship) but it's still a fact. Moral facts are like this — they're relational facts about what agents need to flourish. The relationship to the agent is what makes them moral facts, not what makes them non-facts.

**Assessment:** ProphetDivinityy's distinction is sound in principle but he struggles to articulate it under pressure. The "taller than you" analogy is genuinely good — it shows that relational propositions can be factual. But Wilson keeps pressing "what makes it a fact?" and ProphetDivinityy keeps answering "facts are aspects of reality" without providing the deeper story of *why relational facts are still objective*.

**The missed move (from both sides):** The vault's performative grounding framework resolves this cleanly. ProphetDivinityy needed to say:

> "You're asking me what makes moral facts factual. Here's what: any agent who acts at all is already presupposing that some outcomes are better than others — they demonstrate this by choosing. That's not a stance I'm taking; that's something you prove every time you do anything. The fact that morality is *relational* — that it involves agents — doesn't make it arbitrary, any more than 'taller than' being relational makes height measurements arbitrary."

ProphetDivinityy is essentially making the Gewirthian move (agency → rights) without having the transcendental machinery to seal it. He keeps saying "facts of human nature" and "requirements to flourish" but can't explain *why* those constitute facts without circularity. Performative grounding is exactly the missing piece — it shows that the "factness" of moral claims is grounded in the inescapability of agency itself.

**Vault connection:** [Performative Grounding](../philosophy/morality/performative-grounding-lineage.md) — Gewirth's PGC is the formal version of what ProphetDivinityy is informally arguing. [The Fallacy Fallacy](../philosophy/the-fallacy-fallacy.md) — Wilson repeatedly says "that's tautological" when ProphetDivinityy says "facts are facts," but doesn't explain *why* tautology is a problem here. Is it? The law of identity (A is A) is tautological and foundational.

### 2. The Vikings / Colonization Counterexample

**Wilson's move:** If cooperation always produces better outcomes than conquest, why did the Vikings flourish? Why did European colonizers become a global hegemon by eliminating 99% of native populations?

**ProphetDivinityy's defense:** (a) Slavery limits productive potential — free people produce more. (b) The Vikings were successful *insofar as they were free*, not insofar as they raided. (c) Cooperation would have produced even better outcomes counterfactually. (d) When groups initiate force, using force to stop them is sanctioned by the principle itself.

**Assessment:** This is the hardest exchange for the Objectivists, and ProphetDivinityy handles it unevenly. His core claim — that cooperation produces more total value than conquest — is well-supported by economic theory (comparative advantage, division of labor, [value and profit](../economics/value-and-profit.md)). But his *framing* is vulnerable. When he says it's a "universal truth" that cooperation is always better, Wilson correctly identifies counterexamples where the conquering society *did* benefit pragmatically from conquest, at least in the short to medium term.

**The structural problem:** ProphetDivinityy is conflating two different claims:

1. **Normative:** Cooperation *ought* to be preferred (the moral principle)
2. **Empirical:** Cooperation *always* produces better outcomes (the pragmatic claim)

Claim 1 can be defended on moral grounds even when claim 2 fails. Claim 2 is empirically false in many historical cases — conquest was often pragmatically advantageous for the conqueror. ProphetDivinityy needed to separate these: the moral principle doesn't depend on cooperation always winning. It depends on cooperation being the *right* mode of interaction for beings whose nature is rational agency, regardless of whether any particular act of force happens to produce good consequences for the aggressor.

**The Orthodox Christian interjection** is interesting — this panelist correctly identifies that Wilson is wrong on the economics (citing the "law of continuous dealings" and raider-trade studies), but then gets drawn into a defensive spiral about colonialism. The economic point was sound: repeated cooperative dealings generate more long-term wealth than raiding. But it got lost in emotional crosstalk about whether criticizing colonial conquest is "anti-white."

**Vault connection:** [Value and Profit](../economics/value-and-profit.md) — the mutual surplus argument directly supports cooperation over conquest. [Scope Confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md) — ProphetDivinityy's "universal principle" language is a scope error; the principle is that cooperation is the *morally correct* mode, not that it's empirically optimal in every single case. The timescale problem from the [morality framework](../philosophy/morality/README.md#open-questions) applies: immoral strategies can dominate for generations before error-correction catches up.

### 3. The Children / Grandma Edge Cases

**Wilson's move:** If rights are grounded in the productive use of reason, do children have rights? What about grandma with dementia? If ownership requires applying your mind to reality and producing things, babies and the cognitively impaired can't do that.

**ProphetDivinityy's defense:** Rights develop proportionally with the maturation of rational faculties. Children gradually acquire autonomy as they develop. The principles speak "from the perspective of mankind as a kind" — human nature as species, not any particular individual's current capacity.

**Assessment:** ProphetDivinityy's "kind vs. group" distinction is philosophically substantive (it's a version of the Aristotelian essence argument — human nature defines the *kind*, even if particular instances don't fully realize it). But he introduces it too late and articulates it poorly. Wilson immediately seizes on the apparent gap: if the principle only applies to mature adults, it's not universal.

**The real vulnerability:** This is the same problem that hits every agency-based moral theory, including the vault's performative grounding. If morality is grounded in agency and choice, what moral status do non-agents have (infants, the severely cognitively impaired, the comatose)?

The standard responses:

- **Potentiality** (Aristotelian): The infant is a *potential* agent, and potential agency generates obligations from actual agents.
- **Kind membership** (ProphetDivinityy's move): Human nature defines the kind; individuals participate in the kind's nature even when they can't fully exercise it.
- **Relational obligations** (pragmatic): Actual agents have obligations to non-agents based on the agent's own moral framework — you don't need the baby to be an agent for *you* to have duties regarding the baby.

ProphetDivinityy fumbles between all three without clearly committing to one. The vault's framework would use the third: performative grounding grounds morality in *the agent who acts*, and the acting agent's moral framework includes care for dependents as a convergent norm (virtually every civilization converges on "protect children"). The child doesn't need to be an agent for the parent's agency to generate obligations.

### 4. The "Facts Are Facts" Regress

**Wilson's persistent question:** "What makes a fact a fact?" ProphetDivinityy's persistent answer: "Facts are aspects of reality. Facts don't need to be *made* facts."

This exchange repeats in various forms for easily 15 minutes of the debate and becomes the most frustrating section for both sides. Wilson treats it as evidence that the Objectivist foundation is circular. ProphetDivinityy treats Wilson's question as a category error.

**Assessment:** Both have a point, and both are missing the other's.

**ProphetDivinityy is right that:** Asking "what makes facts factual?" is like asking "what makes existence exist?" — at some point you reach bedrock. The law of identity (A is A) is not a tautology in the pejorative sense; it's the ground floor of rational thought. You cannot explain identity in terms of something more fundamental because there is nothing more fundamental.

**Wilson is right that:** Simply asserting "facts are facts" doesn't help someone who is asking about the *epistemological access* to those facts. Wilson's real question (poorly articulated) is: "How do you *know* these are facts rather than mere beliefs?" ProphetDivinityy hears this as an ontological question about reality and answers with identity; Wilson is asking an epistemological question about justification.

**The vault resolution:** This is a scope confusion between ontology and epistemology — and it's the same confusion that runs through the TAG debates. The ontological question ("do moral facts exist?") and the epistemological question ("how do we know them?") are different questions requiring different answers. ProphetDivinityy's ontological answer is defensible. Wilson's epistemological demand is legitimate. Neither recognizes that they're talking past each other across two different philosophical scopes.

**Vault connection:** [Epistemology parking lot](../philosophy/epistemology/README.md), [Agrippa's trilemma](./agrippa-axioms-wilson-whatever.md) — Wilson's regress ("what makes X? but what makes that?") is Agrippa's infinite regress horn. ProphetDivinityy's "facts are facts" is the dogmatic horn. The vault's performative grounding is the fourth option: you don't justify facts propositionally; you show that denying them while acting is a performative contradiction.

### 5. The Money / Bank Account Example

**ProphetDivinityy's attempt at a stance-independent fact:** "The amount of money in your bank account."

**Wilson's counter:** Bank accounts don't exist without minds. Money's value is stance dependent. If everyone perceives your $300 as $400, the value changes.

**ProphetDivinityy's response:** The *amount* (number of dollars) is stance-independent even though the *purchasing power* is stance-dependent. The origin of something being mind-dependent doesn't make its status as a fact mind-dependent.

**Assessment:** ProphetDivinityy makes a genuinely good philosophical distinction here — between the **causal origin** of a fact and its **status as a fact** — but the example works against him. Bank accounts are paradigmatic social constructions. The number of dollars in your account is only a "fact" within a web of social institutions that depend on minds. He would have been better served using a physical example: "The distance between us is 3 meters" — relational, requires measurement, but the distance exists whether anyone measures it.

Wilson's conflation of "amount of dollars" with "purchasing power" is a genuine equivocation that ProphetDivinityy rightly catches. But the broader point — that bank accounts are mind-dependent artifacts — is hard to overcome with this example.

**The deeper philosophical issue:** ProphetDivinityy is trying to articulate a position that the vault would call **structural realism** — the view that the *patterns* are real even if our *descriptions* of them are constructed. The amount of money in the account is a real pattern in a real system, even though the system is human-made. This is exactly the vault's position on logic: logic is a human construction that models real structure (see [Logic and Mathematics](../philosophy/logic-and-math/README.md)). ProphetDivinityy's instinct is correct; his example selection let him down.

### 6. The Force Principle and Its Defense

**ProphetDivinityy's principle:** Force should only be used to stop force. This is grounded in the requirements of human survival in a social context — initiating force violates the conditions required for flourishing cooperation.

**Wilson's challenges:** (a) What about using force as a deterrent? (b) What makes someone a criminal? (c) Successful societies use lots of force (taxation, regulation) — the most flourishing ones have the most restraints.

**ProphetDivinityy's defense:** The force principle is the precondition for compatible society. Every use of initiated force undermines someone's ability to live productively. Even successful societies that use force are *limiting themselves* — they succeed despite the force, not because of it.

**Assessment:** ProphetDivinityy's defense of the force principle is standard Objectivist/libertarian, and it's reasonably well-argued. The vulnerability is Wilson's point about successful societies using lots of force — this is an empirical challenge that can't be dismissed with theoretical principles alone. The Objectivist position requires showing that these societies would be *even more* successful with less force, which is a counterfactual claim that's inherently hard to demonstrate.

**Vault connection:** [Scope Confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md) — the debate about force is really about scope. ProphetDivinityy's principle (force only to stop force) is the principled gate for law-scope enforcement. Wilson's examples (taxation, regulation) are ethics-to-law promotions that may or may not be legitimate. The scope confusion framework gives ProphetDivinityy better language: "The question isn't whether any society ever uses force successfully. The question is which enforcement mechanism is appropriate for which norms."

## Structural Diagnosis: Why This Debate Goes Nowhere

After 67 minutes, neither side moves the other an inch. The reason is structural:

**Wilson's strategy** is a single-loop regress: "What makes X a fact?" → answer → "What makes *that* a fact?" → answer → repeat. This is Agrippa's infinite regress horn weaponized as a debate tactic. It works against *any* foundational position — theistic or secular — because all foundations eventually bottom out somewhere. Wilson never has to provide his own grounding because he's always on offense.

**ProphetDivinityy's strategy** is repeated assertion of the Objectivist framework (human nature → requirements to flourish → rights) without the transcendental machinery to seal the regress. He keeps saying what the grounding is (human nature) but can't explain *why* that constitutes grounding rather than mere assertion.

**The missing tool for ProphetDivinityy:** Performative grounding. He needs to stop the regress not by asserting "facts are facts" (dogmatism) but by showing that Wilson's act of questioning presupposes the very thing being questioned. Wilson asking "why should I care about human nature?" is an exercise of human nature. Wilson asking "why should I value flourishing?" is a demonstration of valuation. The regress terminates not because you hit a bare assertion, but because the questioner's own activity contradicts the content of their question.

**The missing honesty from Wilson:** Wilson never addresses that his own position faces the identical regress. "What grounds morality?" "God." "What grounds God's moral authority?" "His nature." "What makes God's nature the right ground?" At some point, Wilson bottoms out in assertion too — usually "God is the necessary being" or "God's nature is the ultimate standard." If the Objectivist owes an account of why human nature constitutes grounding, Wilson owes an account of why divine nature does. He never acknowledges this symmetry.

**Vault connection:** [Is God the Necessary Precondition for Knowledge?](./god-precondition-knowledge-nicks-allen.md) — Kurt Allen's "boomerang" move against Eli Nicks is the same structural critique: the theist's regress-stopper (God) faces the same justificatory demands they impose on the secular position. [The Fallacy Fallacy](../philosophy/the-fallacy-fallacy.md) — Wilson labels ProphetDivinityy's responses "tautology" repeatedly without explaining why tautology at the ground floor is a problem rather than a feature.

## Extracted Ideas — What Survives the Noise

1. **Relational facts are still facts.** ProphetDivinityy's "taller than you" analogy is genuinely good. Stance dependence in the relational sense (the fact involves a relationship to an agent) is categorically different from stance dependence in the arbitrary sense (the fact is just someone's opinion). This distinction is essential for the vault's morality framework, which explicitly grounds morality in the interaction between agents and reality — a relational grounding that is not arbitrary.

2. **The origin/status distinction.** ProphetDivinityy's claim that the causal origin of a fact (how it came to exist) is distinct from its status as a fact (whether it's true independent of anyone's wishes) is philosophically sound, even though his bank account example undermined it. A better example: *I own 100 shares of NVIDIA.* That I own 100 shares is a mind-independent fact. What those shares are *worth* is mind-dependent — it fluctuates with millions of minds every trading day. Clean separation, undeniable. Applied to morality: the fact that moral norms were *discovered* by minds doesn't make them *constituted* by minds, any more than the fact that physics was discovered by minds makes it constituted by minds.

3. **Existence doesn't need an interpreter.** Wilson's persistent question — "do facts require minds?" — has a simple answer neither side gave cleanly: *Your parents existed before you did. Your children will be here after you die.* The universe was here before any mind and will be here after the last one. Facts need reality, not interpreters. Interpreters need facts.

4. **The "kind" vs. "group" distinction.** ProphetDivinityy's late-arriving distinction between mankind (the species, the nature) and groups (particular populations) is genuinely important. Moral principles can be true of the *kind* — true of human nature as such — even when particular individuals or groups can't fully realize them. This is the Aristotelian essence position, and it's the correct response to "but babies can't reason" objections.

5. **The animal cognition hole.** PD's definition of a mature adult — "a human being that sustains its life utilizing their reason" — applies to animals. Beavers build dams, crows use tools, squirrels defer consumption. The Objectivist escape ("animals act on instinct, not reason") requires a bright line between instinct and reason that biology doesn't provide — it provides a continuum. Humans have instincts; animals have problem-solving cognition. The same definitional boundary that excludes animals also excludes some humans (life support, severe cognitive impairment), and the same boundary that includes all humans includes some animals.

6. **Labor-mixing fails at the family unit.** PD grounds ownership in "applying mind to nature → keeping what you produce → survival necessity." But the family — the most universal, cross-culturally convergent social structure — operates on shared ownership. A parent produces; an infant consumes. No one tracks individual labor contribution at the dinner table. Monastic communities, kibbutzim, hunter-gatherer bands, partnerships — all function without individual ownership of all production. What's necessary for survival is *some system of resource allocation that participants accept*. Ownership is one such system, and a very good one at scale, but grounding it in survival necessity overclaims.

7. **"Justified" baked into the definition.** PD defines ownership as "the *justified* usage to exclude a scarce good." But justification is the thing being debated — putting it in the definition is circular. This is the same structural error as defining murder as "wrongful killing" and then claiming "murder is wrong" is a moral truth.

8. **Wilson's regress is symmetric.** The most important unspoken fact of the debate: Wilson's "what grounds that?" question applies to every position, including his own. Any grounding Wilson offers for God's moral authority faces the same regress. The only positions that survive Agrippa's trilemma are those that identify a performative ground — something that cannot be denied without the denial being self-undermining.

9. **Cooperation vs. conquest is a timescale question.** The Vikings/colonization exchange reveals that the Objectivist cooperation principle is a *long-run* truth, not a universal short-run truth. Conquest can produce pragmatic gains for the conqueror in the short to medium term. The moral argument against conquest doesn't depend on it always failing pragmatically — it depends on the nature of agency and rights. Conflating the moral argument with the pragmatic argument weakens both. The micro/macro inconsistency is worth noting: people readily accept force at the individual scale (police arrest) but reject the same logic at civilizational scale (conquest). This is a scope question that deserves its own exploration.

10. **The NAP split.** Randian Objectivism wants the non-aggression principle to do everything — ground property, derive rights, justify capitalism, define adulthood, explain human nature. Libertarians use it more modestly as a principled gate: don't initiate force, everything else is negotiable. The libertarian version is more defensible because it's a single enforcement principle, not a metaphysical system. The vault's position is closest to the libertarian usage: the NAP is the principled gate for *law-scope* enforcement specifically, not the whole of morality.

## Connection to Saturday's Debate (2026-03-14)

**Topic:** "You cannot be moral if you are not religious."

Several patterns from this debate apply directly:

1. **Wilson's regress tactic** is likely to appear from theistic opponents: "But what grounds *that*? But what makes *that* a fact?" The [debate prep sheet](../../notes/debate-prep-morality-religion-2026-03-14.md) already has the performative grounding counter, but this debate shows how relentless the regress pressure can be. Practice the one-sentence shutdown: "My own actions ground it. Yours too. You can't ask 'why should I value?' without valuing."

2. **The "stance dependent = not real" move** will likely recur. Be ready to distinguish relational from arbitrary: "Is 'taller than you' not a fact because it's relational? Morality is relational too — it involves agents and their nature — but relational doesn't mean made up."

3. **The cooperation argument is a trap.** Don't argue that morality is grounded in cooperation producing better outcomes. This opens the Vikings/conquest counterexample. Instead: morality is grounded in agency itself — choosing agents are moral by definition — and cooperation is a *consequence* of moral agency, not the *ground* of it.

4. **Don't get stuck in "facts are facts."** ProphetDivinityy's mistake was answering the regress with bare identity assertions. The prep sheet's "concede and win" move is better: "I'll grant you God for free. Now tell me — does a choosing agent still need to act? Then morality still comes from agency, not from who made the agent."

## Broader Vault Connections

- **[Performative Grounding](../philosophy/morality/performative-grounding-lineage.md):** This debate is a case study in what happens when someone holds the right intuition (morality grounded in human nature and agency) but lacks the transcendental machinery to defend it against regress. ProphetDivinityy is essentially a proto-Gewirthian who hasn't read Gewirth.

- **[Self-Ownership and Property Rights](../philosophy/legal-theory/self-ownership-and-property-rights.md):** ProphetDivinityy's ownership argument (ownership originates from applying mind to nature, which is required for survival) faces the same liberty-right/claim-right problem identified in Hoppe's argumentation ethics, plus the family-unit counterexample kills the "survival necessity" claim. Vault conclusion: self-ownership and property rights may be better grounded in conflict avoidance than in productive agency. **Property rights needs its own dedicated vault page** — labor-mixing, conflict avoidance, the family counterexample, and the "justified" circularity all need formal treatment.

- **[Scope Confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md):** Multiple scope confusions operate simultaneously: ontology vs. epistemology (what exists vs. how we know it), individual vs. species (ProphetDivinityy's kind/group), the enforceability question (when is force legitimate?), and the micro/macro force inconsistency (accepting police violence but rejecting colonial violence as the same principle at different scale).

- **[Economics — Value and Profit](../economics/value-and-profit.md):** The cooperation-vs-conquest exchange is the moral version of the economic argument for trade. Both rest on the claim that voluntary exchange produces mutual surplus. The economic version is less vulnerable to counterexample because it makes a probabilistic long-run claim, not a universal claim about every case.

- **[The Gödel Governance Problem](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md):** Wilson's regress exposes that *every* moral system ultimately rests on something that can't be further justified within the system. This is the moral version of Gödel: any system powerful enough to ground morality is powerful enough to generate unanswerable "but why?" questions about its own foundations. The only escape is performative grounding — showing that the questioner is already inside the system by virtue of asking.

## Open Questions

1. ~~Can the "taller than you" analogy be formalized?~~ **Resolved.** See [Relational Objectivity](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md) — the four-category ontology (intrinsic, relational mind-independent, agent-relative, mind-dependent) formalizes ProphetDivinityy's intuition. Agent-relative facts are relational and objective; the "taller than" analogy is actually category 2 (relational mind-independent), and moral facts are category 3 (agent-relative). Both are objective; adding agents to the relation doesn't degrade factuality.
2. How does the Objectivist "kind vs. group" distinction relate to the vault's scope model? Is "mankind as kind" a scope level, and if so, where does it sit on the enforceability gradient?
3. Wilson never offers his own positive account of moral grounding in this debate. In previous Wilson debates (see the [three-part series](./agrippa-axioms-wilson-whatever.md)), he defaults to "God's nature is the standard." How does this compare to ProphetDivinityy's "human nature is the standard"? Both are nature-based groundings — the question is whose nature and why.
4. **Property rights grounding.** Labor-mixing (Locke → Rothbard → Objectivism) fails at the family unit and bakes "justified" into the definition. Conflict avoidance is the vault's leading candidate. Needs its own page with formal treatment.
5. **Trade vs. conquest economics.** The Orthodox Christian panelist claimed trade always beats conquest. Empirically false in many cases (Vikings, Aztecs, colonial empires). But the micro/macro force inconsistency — accepting police violence while rejecting the same logic at civilizational scale — is a scope question worth exploring.
6. **The instinct/reason bright line.** Objectivism requires a categorical distinction between animal instinct and human reason. Biology provides a continuum. Where does this leave animal moral status, and does it threaten every agency-based moral framework (including the vault's)?
