## Context

Tim Pool debates Kyla on who should vote. Pool argues for stakeholder-based suffrage — only those with skin in the game (landowners, taxpayers, informed citizens) should decide policy. Kyla agrees suffrage should require stakeholdership but argues that being a citizen over 18 is sufficient. Jake's review sides with Pool. All three miss the structural issue.

## The Debate Positions

### Tim Pool: Restrict the Franchise

Pool's argument:
1. Uninformed voters are bad for the system
2. Democratic activists register clueless young people at concerts who then get told how to vote
3. The founding fathers required stakeholdership (land ownership) for a reason
4. The 17th Amendment (direct election of senators) degraded the founders' vision
5. Being 18 and a citizen is not sufficient stakeholdership

**What he gets right:** There IS a problem with mobilizing uninformed voters as a political strategy. Ballot harvesting among people who "don't know and don't care" degrades the quality of democratic input.

**What he gets wrong:** His solution — restricting the franchise — doesn't fix the underlying problem and creates new ones.

### Kyla: Universal Suffrage with Stakeholdership

Kyla's argument:
1. The founders used land ownership as a proxy for stakeholdership because it fit their era
2. They were "always open" that suffrage criteria would update as the country matured
3. Today, being a citizen over 18 IS sufficient stakeholdership
4. Any restriction needs to ensure everyone "feels fairly treated"

**What she gets right:** Stakeholdership criteria evolve with the society. The founders restricted the franchise to landowning white men not because that's the eternal truth but because it was their best proxy for "people with skin in the game" in 1789.

**What she gets wrong:** The "everyone should feel fairly treated" framing is exactly the wrong standard for governance. Policy isn't about managing feelings — it's about producing sustainable outcomes. Jake correctly identifies this as scaling a family-level ethic to national governance.

**What she missed entirely:** She should have pushed back on Pool's premise rather than conceding it. The correct response to "should uninformed people vote?" isn't "well, we need to make it fair." It's "being uninformed doesn't remove your stake."

## The Stakeholdership Fallacy

### Uninformed ≠ Unstaked

A clueless 18-year-old at a concert who can't name the capital of their state still:
- Lives under the laws that get passed
- Pays sales tax on everything they buy
- Can be drafted in wartime
- Faces the criminal justice system
- Will work in an economy shaped by policy decisions
- Breathes air regulated (or not) by environmental policy
- Drives on roads funded by tax allocation decisions

They have a stake. They don't have competence. These are different problems with different solutions.

**Stake** = your life is affected by the outcome. This is binary — either you're subject to the government's authority or you aren't. If you are, you have a stake. Period.

**Competence** = you understand the issues well enough to vote wisely. This is a gradient — nobody is fully competent on all issues, and "competent enough" is a judgment call that someone has to make.

Restricting the franchise conflates these two by saying "if you're not competent, you have no stake." But competence and stake are independent variables:

| | Has stake | No stake |
|---|----------|---------|
| **Competent** | Should obviously vote | Why are they voting on something that doesn't affect them? |
| **Not competent** | Has a right to participate in decisions that affect their life | Not relevant |

The "informed voter" argument lives in the top-left box and wants to exclude the bottom-left. But everyone in the bottom-left is still AFFECTED by the outcome. Denying them voice doesn't remove their stake — it just means their stake gets managed by someone else without their consent.

### Tyranny Doesn't Disappear with Restricted Suffrage

Every restriction on the franchise just changes who exercises the tyranny of the majority:

| Electorate | Historical period | What they voted for | Who got screwed |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Landowning white men | 1789–early 1800s | Policies favoring landowners, maintained slavery | Non-landowners, women, enslaved people |
| White men (no land requirement) | Jacksonian era onward | Populist policies, maintained racial exclusion | Black Americans, women, indigenous peoples |
| All men | Post-15th Amendment | Policies favoring male economic interests | Women (no property rights in many states until married women's property acts) |
| All adults | Post-19th Amendment, fully post-Voting Rights Act | Policies favoring the median voter | Minorities, the wealthy, anyone outside the median |

The pattern: the enfranchised group votes for its own interests. Always. Landowners voted for landowner-friendly policies. Men voted for male-friendly policies. Expanding the franchise didn't create self-interested voting — it was already there. It just changed whose self-interest dominated.

Tim Pool's Fred — the 62-year-old landowner with five kids who "knows how the government works" — would vote for policies that benefit 62-year-old landowners with five kids. That's not wisdom. It's just a different set of self-interested preferences, backed by more life experience but no less self-serving.

### The Competence Problem Proves Too Much

If "uninformed people shouldn't vote" is the principle, where does it stop?

- Pool's 62-year-old Fred "knows how the government works." Can he explain the Federal Reserve's balance sheet? Probably not. Should he vote on monetary policy?
- Can Fred explain how the commerce clause expanded from regulating interstate trade to regulating a farmer growing wheat for his own consumption (Wickard v. Filburn, 1942)? Probably not. Should he vote on regulatory policy?
- Can Fred evaluate whether a military budget of $850 billion is the right number given the threat from China? Almost certainly not. Should he vote on defense spending?

The honest answer is that NOBODY is competent on the full range of issues a modern government controls. The system has grown so complex that informed voting on all of it is impossible for any individual. Pool's "informed voter" standard, applied consistently, would eliminate everyone including Pool himself.

## The Real Problem: Scope

The vault's consistent position, from the [SAVE Act page](./save-act-suffrage-hoppian.md) through the [Gödel Governance Problem](../philosophy/the-godel-governance-problem.md):

**The damage from bad voting is proportional to what the vote controls.**

### Narrow scope (the founders' design):

- Federal government handles defense, treaties, interstate commerce, currency
- States handle most governance
- Enumerated powers limit what representatives can do
- Even a terrible representative can't do much damage because the system constrains them
- Voter competence matters less because the consequences of any single bad vote are bounded

### Vast scope (current reality):

- Federal government controls healthcare, education, monetary policy, environmental regulation, social welfare, military operations across the globe, technology regulation, housing policy, agricultural subsidies, and on and on
- A single election changes policy across all of these domains simultaneously
- No voter — not Pool's Fred, not anyone — is competent to evaluate all of this
- Bad votes cascade through everything because nothing constrains the consequences

The founders didn't restrict the franchise primarily because they distrusted the people. They restricted the **scope of government** because they distrusted **power.** The franchise restrictions (landowning white men) reflected the biases of their era. The structural restrictions (enumerated powers, separation of powers, federalism, the Bill of Rights) were the real safeguard.

What eroded wasn't the franchise — that expanded, and rightly so. What eroded was the scope constraints:
- The commerce clause expanded from interstate trade to virtually everything (Wickard, Gonzales v. Raich)
- The 17th Amendment removed the states' check on federal power
- The New Deal expanded federal authority into domains the founders never contemplated
- Post-9/11 expanded executive power beyond legislative or judicial check

The franchise expanded while the scope constraints collapsed. Everyone argues about the first and ignores the second. But the second is what actually determines how much damage bad voting can do.

## The Agency Argument

There is a deeper problem with restricting suffrage that neither Pool nor Jake addresses:

**Telling someone "you don't get a say in how you're governed" is denying their agency.**

If you are subject to a government's laws — if it can tax you, draft you, imprison you, regulate your behavior — then you have a moral claim to participate in choosing that government. This isn't a pragmatic argument about good outcomes. It's a rights argument about self-governance.

A person governed without consent is a subject, not a citizen. The distinction between "citizen" and "subject" is precisely the right to participate in governance. Remove that right and you haven't improved democracy — you've abandoned it.

Even a bad vote is an exercise of agency. The 18-year-old at the concert who votes without understanding the issues is making a choice about their own governance. It might be a stupid choice. But it's THEIR choice about THEIR life. Taking it away because someone else judges them insufficiently informed is paternalism — deciding for someone else what's best for them.

**The solution to bad voting isn't removing the vote.** It's:
1. **Reduce scope** — limit what the vote controls so bad choices have bounded consequences
2. **Improve education** — make voters more competent (though this has its own problems — who decides what "informed" means?)
3. **Structural safeguards** — separation of powers, federalism, enumerated powers, judicial review — all designed to buffer bad voting, not eliminate it

## Vault Connection: The Deontology Bridge

This connects to the [Deontology vs Consequentialism](../philosophy/morality/deontology-consequentialism.md) page. Pool's argument is consequentialist — restrict the franchise because uninformed voting produces bad outcomes. The agency argument is deontological — people have a right to participate in their own governance regardless of the outcomes.

The vault's resolution: the deontological principle (right to vote) is itself a cached consequentialist result. Societies that deny agency to governed populations produce worse long-term outcomes than those that include them — because the excluded eventually revolt, and because the included have information about their own needs that no external judge can capture. The right to vote isn't just a moral principle — it's the institutionalized version of "the governed know things about their own lives that the governors don't."

This is the Hayekian knowledge problem applied to governance: no central authority can aggregate the preferences and local knowledge of millions of people. Voting is the (imperfect) mechanism for doing so. Restricting the franchise doesn't improve the aggregation — it blinds the system to the preferences and knowledge of the excluded.

## Open Questions

1. **Is there a role for weighted voting?** Not restricting WHO votes but weighting votes differently — e.g., net taxpayers get slightly more weight, or votes are weighted by how many dependents you support. This preserves universal suffrage while addressing the stakeholdership concern. Problems: who designs the weights? (Weighting Problem again.)
2. **Should there be a competence floor without restricting the vote?** Mandatory civics education, or requiring voters to pass a basic knowledge test before each election (not to qualify — everyone votes — but the test score weights the vote). This is Singapore's approach to some extent. Deeply problematic in practice (who writes the test?) but structurally interesting.
3. **Is scope reduction politically achievable?** The vault says "restrict scope, not the vote." But scope has only expanded for 235 years. Can it actually contract? DOGE is the current experiment. Historical precedent is not encouraging — [civilizational cycles](../philosophy/civilizational-cycles.md) suggest scope expands until collapse.
4. **Does digital democracy change the calculus?** If voters could vote issue-by-issue rather than choosing representatives who decide everything, would the competence problem be more tractable? You'd only need to be informed on the issues you choose to vote on.
