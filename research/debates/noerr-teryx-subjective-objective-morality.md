## Context

A repeat matchup. Rob has debated this topic before (see [previous Noerr debate](./noerr-logic-feelings-humane.md)). The stated topic is whether morality is subjective or objective. Both agree objective morality exists. The fight is over whether Terry's morality qualifies as objective.

Quality is low — frequent interruptions, insults, cross-talk. But the structural failure is instructive.

## Argument Structures

### Rob Noerr (Morality is subjective without divine revelation)
1. Do you operate through a code of morality? (Yes)
2. Do you know what objective morality is? (No)
3. Therefore your day-to-day moral decisions are subjective — you're guessing.
4. Believing an answer exists doesn't make your guesses objective.
5. A Christian/Muslim can at least point to a source they believe is God's word. You can't point to anything.

**Key analogy:** "I'm working on a car. Should I unscrew this nut first or this one? I don't know, I'm just going to guess. But I know there IS an answer. That doesn't mean I'm doing what's right — I'm subjectively deciding."

### Terry X (Morality is objective via logic)
1. Objective morality must exist — denying it is self-refuting ("to say there is no truth is itself a truth claim").
2. Logic and empirical evidence are objective systems.
3. I'm pointing to an objective system, therefore my morality is objective.
4. If we agree objective truth exists, and morality is truth, then morality is objective. QED.

## Analysis

### The Two-Category Trap

Both debaters operate within a two-category model:

| Category | Label | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Mind-independent | "1 + 1 = 2" |
| Subjective | Mind-dependent | "Chocolate is better than vanilla" |

They agree objective morality exists (category 1). The fight is over whether Terry *accesses* it. But the two-category model makes this impossible for either side:

- **Rob's problem:** If objective morality requires divine revelation, then morality isn't really mind-independent — it's communicated *to* minds *by* a mind (God's). Rob is smuggling minds in through the back door.
- **Terry's problem:** He can't bridge from "logic exists" to "therefore stealing is wrong." When pressed, he says "because you can multiply and divide," which is gibberish. He gestures at an objective system but can't walk across the is-ought gap.

### The Broken Definition

Both accept: **"objective" = "exists without humans."**

But morality is about **action**. Action requires **a mind**. So "mind-independent morality" is incoherent:
- Can you steal without a mind?
- Can you murder without a mind?
- Can you betray without a mind?

The demand for mind-independent morality is like demanding mind-independent hunger. Hunger is real, objective in every meaningful sense, you can be wrong about what satisfies it — but remove all organisms and hunger disappears. That doesn't make hunger "subjective."

### What Neither Side Sees: Category 3

The vault's [Relational Objectivity](../philosophy/epistemology/relational-objectivity.md) framework resolves this with a four-category ontology:

| Category | Requires minds? | Arbitrary? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Intrinsic | No | No | Mass of the sun |
| 2. Relational mind-independent | No | No | Tree A taller than Tree B |
| **3. Agent-relative** | **Yes** | **No** | **Cyanide is poisonous; betrayal isolates** |
| 4. Mind-dependent | Yes | Yes | "Chocolate is better than vanilla" |

Moral facts live in **category 3**: they require agents to exist, but given agents, the facts are determined by the structure of reality, not by opinion. You can be *wrong* about them. Independent civilizations *converge* on the core norms. Reality produces *differential outcomes* (cooperative societies persist; betrayal-based ones collapse).

**Terry needed category 3** — morality that's objective because it's constrained by reality, even though it requires minds. Instead he kept asserting "logic exists, therefore my morality is objective" without ever explaining the mechanism.

**Rob's argument is structurally sound within the two-category model** — if the only options are mind-independent or subjective, and Terry can't access the mind-independent truth, then Terry's morality is subjective. But the two-category model is wrong, so Rob wins the debate while being wrong about the deeper question.

### The Scope Confusion

Terry commits textbook [scope confusion](../philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md): he conflates the *metaphysical* claim ("objective morality exists") with the *epistemological* claim ("I know what it is") and the *practical* claim ("my decisions are objective"). These are three different scopes:

1. **Metaphysical:** There exist moral facts. (Both agree.)
2. **Epistemological:** We can discover them through logic. (Terry asserts, can't demonstrate.)
3. **Practical:** My day-to-day choices track those facts. (Terry assumes, Rob denies.)

Rob correctly identifies that (1) doesn't entail (3). Terry never engages with this distinction.

### Logic and Math Are Also Mind-Dependent

Terry's fallback is that logic and math are objective systems that ground morality. But the vault's [logic series](../philosophy/logic-and-math/README.md) shows these are themselves more complex than "mind-independent":

- Multiple logics exist (classical, intuitionistic, quantum, paraconsistent) — which is "the" objective one?
- Formal systems are *constructed* by minds — the patterns they describe may be real, but the formalisms are human artifacts.
- Gödel's incompleteness means no formal system captures all mathematical truth.

This doesn't make logic subjective — it makes it **category 3**, like morality. The patterns are real and constrained by reality. The formalisms require minds. "Logic is objective" is true in the agent-relative sense, not in the mind-independent sense.

Terry accidentally stumbles near this insight ("logic is also the book of God") but can't articulate it.

## Structural Problems

**Terry:**
- Cannot bridge from "logic exists" to any specific moral claim
- Confuses "pointing to an objective system" with "using that system to derive conclusions"
- Brings up the Constitution as an example of "objective truth" — a document written by humans through compromise, amended 27 times. This undermines his position rather than supporting it.
- Resorts to "A cannot equal not-A" as if the law of non-contradiction settles moral questions

**Rob:**
- Structurally sound within his framework, but the framework (two-category + divine revelation) is limited
- Assumes the only path to objective morality is through specific religious texts — dismisses the possibility of rational moral discovery
- Gets baited into personal attacks that weaken his presentation
- Never offers his own account of HOW divine revelation makes morality objective — just asserts it does

## Open Questions

- Does Rob's position reduce to divine command theory? If God declared murder moral, would it be? He never addresses this, but his framework implies it.
- Could Terry's argument be rescued by replacing "logic tells me stealing is wrong" with an actual derivation? The performative grounding lineage suggests yes — but it requires philosophical machinery Terry doesn't have.
