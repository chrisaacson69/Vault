Interesting question. I think Mises is making a methodological distinction, not a biological one. He's not saying animals don't have motivated behavior — he acknowledges the "borderline" in the footnote. He's saying we can only understand (verstehen) behavior by imputing motives we ourselves can comprehend.

So his argument is really about the limits of praxeological method: we can deduce the logical structure of human action because we share the same reasoning apparatus. With animals, we can observe patterns but can't access the internal logic — we're projecting our own framework onto them.

The weakness is that this draws an overly sharp line. A chimpanzee choosing between two food sources based on quantity, or a crow solving a multi-step puzzle to get a reward — these look a lot like purposeful action with means-end reasoning. Mises would say we're imputing that logic, not knowing it. But the same could be said of understanding other humans — we're always imputing motives based on our own experience.

He may have been constrained by the zoological knowledge of his time (1949). The evidence for higher animal cognition, tool use, and even economic-like behavior (trading, fairness preferences in primates) has grown enormously since then.
