# Debate Prep: "You Cannot Be Moral If You Are Not Religious"
> Negative position — you CAN be moral without religion.

**Date:** Saturday, 2026-03-14 @ 6 PM PST
**Resolution:** "You cannot be moral if you are not religious."
**Your side:** Negative (against the resolution)

---

## Core Thesis

Choosing agents are moral by definition. The act of existing and making decisions is inescapably normative — agency IS morality at its foundation. Religion can enrich moral life, but it is not a prerequisite. Christianity's own tradition supports this.

---

## Opening Framework

1. **Agency grounds morality.** If you exist and must act, you must choose. Choosing requires evaluation. Evaluation IS normative. You're doing morality the moment you deliberate — no scripture required.

2. **Convergent norms are structural, not commanded.** Every culture independently converges on "don't murder, don't steal, don't betray" — not because they all received the same revelation, but because the structure of agency is the same everywhere. These norms are the minimum conditions for agents to coexist.

3. **This is compatible with religion.** Showing morality doesn't require religion is NOT showing God doesn't exist. A theist can fully accept this framework and say "God created agents, therefore God created moral beings." The framework is God-optional, not anti-God.

---

## Anticipated Attacks & Responses

### Attack 1: "Choice doesn't mean GOOD — where's your standard?"

**Their move:** "A serial killer chooses. Choice alone doesn't give you good and bad. You need a standard."

**Your response:**
- Agency isn't just choice — it's choice with built-in structural constraints.
- Some choices preserve the conditions for future choice-making (cooperation, honesty, trade). Some destroy them (murder, theft, betrayal).
- The standard isn't external — it's embedded in agency's structure. Convergent norms emerge because the structure of agency is universal.
- The serial killer is a *defective* agent — his choices undermine the cooperative framework his own survival depends on. That's not imported from outside. It's a structural observation.

**If they press "but WHY is preserving agency good?":**
- Performative contradiction: you're exercising agency to question agency's value. The regression terminates.
- Neutralizer: "Every framework hits a foundation. Yours hits 'God's nature is good — it just is.' Mine hits 'agency is inescapably normative — you can't question it without demonstrating it.' Neither avoids a foundation. But mine is demonstrable without faith. Yours requires it. That proves the resolution wrong — morality is accessible without religion."

### Attack 2: "God created the universe — His rules apply"

**Their move:** "Without God, there's no universe for you to exist in. You live within His creation, therefore His moral framework."

**Your response — Euthyphro dilemma:**
- Is something good *because* God commands it? → Morality is arbitrary. God could command murder and it would be "good." That's obedience, not morality.
- Does God command it *because* it's good? → The standard exists independent of God. You don't need God to access it.
- Either morality is arbitrary or God is unnecessary for it. Pick one.

**Your response — constraints ≠ morality:**
- God may have created the constraints (physics, biology, the action space). But morality emerges from agency WITHIN those constraints, not from the constraint-setter's authority.
- You don't need to know who designed the system to act morally within it. You just need to be an agent.
- If "creator = moral authority" holds, then ANY sufficiently powerful creator's commands would be moral regardless of content. That reduces morality to power, which is obedience, not morality.

**The warden analogy** is a forcing line — it makes them prove God exists rather than assume it. You probably won't need it because the maker/actor distinction (above) lets you concede God's existence for free and still win the point. Save the warden for the case where they explicitly refuse to argue without presupposing God — then it forces the question. Otherwise, conceding the maker is stronger because it takes away their implicit assumption without a fight.

**If they try "God IS good — goodness flows from His nature":**
- That makes "good" a brute fact about God's nature. Why is God's nature good? "It just is." That's the dogmatic horn of Agrippa's trilemma — an assertion without justification. It's faith, not argument. Fine as faith — but it doesn't prove morality REQUIRES God.

**The "concede and win" move:**
- They will likely assume God's existence in every attack without stating it. Don't fight this — concede it freely: "I'll grant you a maker for the sake of argument. It doesn't matter. The maker sets constraints. Morality comes from agents acting within those constraints. You don't need to know the maker to know murder is wrong."
- This is your strongest play: you've given them God for free and still won the point. The debate stays on morality, not cosmology. If they can't win WITH God conceded, they can't win at all.

### Attack 3: "Without God, everything is permitted" (Dostoevsky)

**Their move:** "If there's no God, there's no ultimate accountability. People can do anything."

**Your response:**
- This confuses morality with enforcement. People CAN do immoral things whether God exists or not. The question is whether they can KNOW it's immoral without religion.
- Agency-grounded morality provides the knowledge: you know murder is wrong because it destroys the conditions of agency, not because you fear punishment.
- The "accountability" argument actually undermines their position: if the only reason to be moral is fear of divine punishment, that's not morality — it's self-interest. A person who refrains from murder only because God is watching is LESS moral than one who refrains because they understand why it's wrong.

### Attack 4: "Moral relativism without God"

**Their move:** "Without an absolute standard (God), morality is just opinion. My morality vs. yours."

**Your response:**
- Agency-grounded morality is NOT relativist. The convergent norms are universal because agency is universal. "Don't murder" isn't opinion — it's structural.
- The margins ARE subjective (scope confusion — where we draw lines on enforcement). But the core is convergent across every culture, religion, and time period.
- Their own position has the same problem: WHICH God? WHICH interpretation? Christians disagree on divorce, war, capital punishment, homosexuality. Having a God doesn't eliminate moral disagreement — it just moves the disagreement to interpretation.

## The Christian Tradition (DEFENSIVE USE ONLY)

**Do NOT lead with scripture.** Quoting verses offensively as a non-theist reads as bad-faith and invites counter-quotes from someone who knows the text better than you. Keep this in your back pocket — use it ONLY if they cite scripture first, or not at all.

**The safer move — reference the tradition, not the text:**

"The natural law tradition within Christianity — Aquinas being the most prominent example — holds that moral truths are accessible through reason alone, without revelation. Your own intellectual tradition has a well-developed framework that agrees morality doesn't require religion."

This is harder to counter-quote because you're referencing a philosophical tradition, not a specific verse they can contextualize away.

### If THEY cite scripture and you need to respond:

| Source | What it says | Use ONLY if |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Romans 2:14-15** | Gentiles have the law "written on their hearts" | They claim scripture says morality requires faith |
| **The Good Samaritan** (Luke 10:25-37) | Jesus holds up the non-religious outsider as the moral example | They claim Jesus taught morality requires religion |
| **Common Grace** (Reformed/Calvinist) | God's grace enables moral behavior in non-believers | They claim their denomination denies morality without faith |

### The safe framing (if you use any of this):

"I'm not here to interpret your scripture — you know it better than I do. But I'd note that the natural law tradition, which is central to Christian philosophy, holds that moral knowledge is accessible through reason. That's not my claim — that's Aquinas."

**Key principle:** Let THEM open the scripture door. If they don't, you don't need it — the philosophical argument stands on its own.

## Closing Framework

"The resolution says you CANNOT be moral without religion. I've shown three things:

1. Agency grounds morality without requiring religion — choosing agents are moral by definition, and convergent norms emerge from the structure of agency itself.

2. The strongest arguments for requiring God — divine command and creation authority — either make morality arbitrary or make God unnecessary for it.

3. Even within the Christian intellectual tradition, natural law holds that moral truths are accessible through reason alone. The resolution is a stronger claim than the tradition itself supports.

You don't need religion to be moral. Religion may deepen, enrich, or motivate moral life — and that's valuable. But the resolution claims you CANNOT be moral without it. That claim fails on philosophical grounds and empirical grounds — and it overshoots what even the strongest theistic moral frameworks require."
