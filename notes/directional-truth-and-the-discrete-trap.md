# Directional Truth and the Discrete Trap
> We want discrete destinations ("no government") when reality only supports directional vectors ("less government"). The discrete framing overshoots the actual truth AND triggers the opposing force. Movements need a banner to rally around, so directional insights get packaged as destinations; the destinations are almost always false even when the directions are right.

**Links:** [Weaponized Nuance](./weaponized-nuance.md) (mirror-pair — under-committed vs. over-committed failures of reasoning-about-general-claims), [Opposing Forces](../research/philosophy/dynamics/opposing-forces.md), [The Constitutive/Elective Distinction](../research/philosophy/morality/constitutive-elective.md), [Scope Confusion](../research/philosophy/morality/scope-confusion.md), [The Weighting Problem](../research/philosophy/epistemology/weighting-problem.md), [Lance Bush Reviews Wilson on Triggernometry](../research/debates/lance-bush-review-wilson-triggernometry.md) (the metaethics instance of the same pattern), [HoP Ep 15 — Aristotle's Logic](../research/philosophy/tangents/history-of-philosophy/15-aristotle-logic.md)

## The pattern

Most propositions that feel like "X is the answer" are actually "move toward X" dressed up as destinations. The discrete form is what you rally around; the directional form is what's true. The mismatch is structural — language and argumentation strongly prefer discrete positions, but the underlying reality is usually a gradient with a correct direction rather than a fixed correct point.

When the discrete position is asserted:
- It **overshoots the truth** — the destination is false even though the vector pointing at it is right
- It **triggers the opposing force** — the extreme statement generates its opposite (Opposing Forces)
- It **makes the movement legible** — which is why movements keep doing it; you can't rally around a vector as easily as a destination

## Canonical cases

- **Ancap / libertarianism.** "No government" is the discrete destination; "much less government than we currently have" is the directional truth. Most libertarian arguments actually support the direction and overreach to the destination.
- **Socialism / communism.** "No private property" destination; "more public provisioning in domains where markets fail" direction.
- **Anti-monopoly.** "Break them up" destination; "reduce concentration where capture hasn't yet occurred" direction.
- **Traditionalism.** "Restore the past" destination; "preserve more continuity than modernity would otherwise allow" direction.
- **Utilitarianism.** "Maximize aggregate utility" destination (with [its known failure modes](../research/debates/utilitarianism-sheriff-counterexample.md)); "weight utility more heavily in decisions than intuition alone would" direction.
- **Open borders / closed borders.** Symmetric destinations; the directional truth is "adjust migration policy in response to integration capacity" — context-dependent.
- **Free speech absolutism.** "No limits" destination; "limits should be very narrow and structurally-motivated" direction.

In each case, the movement's banner is the destination; the defensible argument is the direction; the destination attracts the opposing force; the direction is where the actual policy lies.

## Aristotle got there first

Aristotle's doctrine of the mean is the clean historical example of handling this well. Virtue is the directional mean between two vices — courage is the direction away from both cowardice and recklessness; temperance is the direction away from both indulgence and asceticism. The virtue *word* is the vector; the vice words name the extremes. The mean is context-dependent, not a fixed midpoint.

This is the move Aristotelian ethics makes explicit and most political ideologies fail to make. Aristotle treats the discrete labels (virtue names) as vocabulary for the direction, not for a destination. A libertarian following the virtue model would say "government-minimalism" as a directional virtue bounded by "tyranny" on one side and "anarchy" on the other — neither extreme is the goal; the goal is to move away from both, and where you should be depends on where you currently are.

## What this is not yet

Open questions that would flesh this out:
- Is there a principled distinction between cases where the directional reading is correct and cases where the destination reading is correct? (Constitutive features might be the exception — they really do have a destination.)
- Is the discrete-framing problem solvable at the political-communication level, or is it an unavoidable cost of coalition-forming?
- Does the pattern have a formal statement? Something like: *if the reward function is monotonic in X, the policy that maximizes it is directional in X — but treating the directional policy as a destination introduces a second-order penalty via opposing forces.*

Examples that feel like this pattern but haven't been placed yet can get filed here as they come up. The point of the note is to have a container, not to be complete.
